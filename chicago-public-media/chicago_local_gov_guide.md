## The Political Landscape

Mayor Brandon Johnson governs a city where the council majority has turned against him on fiscal policy. The clearest fault line runs through the budget: Johnson proposed a corporate head tax that the council rejected, and a bloc of aldermen led by Scott Waguespack (32nd Ward), Gilbert Villegas (36th Ward), and Samantha Nugent (39th Ward) is now pushing an alternative revenue plan built on advertising sales, debt collection, and new taxes on cloud computing and plastic bags ([City Council majority turns up the heat on Mayor Brandon Johnson to implement alternative budget](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/11/chicago-city-council-majority-turns-up-the-heat-on-mayor-brandon-johnson-to-implement-alternative-budget)). Johnson warned of a possible mid-year shortfall. This is not a theoretical dispute — the city already split a $260 million advance pension payment because of a cash-flow crunch tied to delayed Cook County property tax receipts ([Mayoral aides explain Chicago's cash flow crunch that triggered partial pension payment](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/09/cash-flow-crunch-partial-pension-payment-mayor-brandon-johnson)).

Johnson's relationship with the council plays out issue by issue. He vetoed a hemp-product ban pushed by Ald. Marty Quinn (13th Ward), and the council fell eight votes short of overriding him ([Hemp ban dies after City Council upholds Mayor Johnson's veto](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/18/hemp-ban-brandon-johnson-veto-override-attempt-defeated-chicago-city-council)). ## Major Issues and Ongoing Battles

### 1. The Budget War

The city's finances dominate this coverage. After the council rejected Johnson's corporate head tax, the alternative-budget working groups are exploring revenue from advertising on city assets, selling long-outstanding debt, a cloud computing tax, and a plastic bag tax ([City Council majority turns up the heat](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/11/chicago-city-council-majority-turns-up-the-heat-on-mayor-brandon-johnson-to-implement-alternative-budget)). Johnson's camp countered by blaming a cash-flow crunch on delayed Cook County property tax receipts and a $1 billion TIF surplus earmarked for CPS, while assuring that splitting the $260 million pension advance wouldn't hurt the city's bond rating ([Mayoral aides explain Chicago's cash flow crunch](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/09/cash-flow-crunch-partial-pension-payment-mayor-brandon-johnson)).

Meanwhile, business license fees jumped by as much as 400%, blindsiding small businesses ([Business license fees increased this year, some by as high as 400%](https://chicago.suntimes.com/small-business/2026/02/23/business-license-fees-increased-chicago)). A court ruling that the city overcharged motorists on sticker and parking fines — violating a state $250 cap — could force Chicago to refund up to $104 million plus $34 million in interest ([City sticker violations overcharges](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/24/chicago-overcharge-city-sticker-violations-parking-tickets-fine-cap-cook-county-ruling-refunds)). The Chicago Federation of Labor pushed back against the Civic Federation for suggesting furloughs and cuts to city workers ([Civic Federation scapegoats city workers](https://chicago.suntimes.com/letters-to-the-editor/2026/02/07/civic-federation-city-workers-epstein-school-vouchers-kennedy-center-disabled-taxes-cps-protests)). And hundreds of firefighters and paramedics still haven't received four years of retroactive pay because of payroll errors, with interest accruing at 4.5% annually ([Mistakes stall back pay for hundreds of Chicago firefighters and paramedics](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/13/back-pay-chicago-chicago-firefighters-paramedics-administrative-mistakes)).

The Finance Committee rejected an $8.25 million settlement for the family of a woman killed in a police chase, with aldermen arguing the city shouldn't bear the cost ([Finance Committee rejects $8.25M settlement](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/11/finance-committee-rejects-8-25m-settlement-to-compensate-family-of-woman-killed-in-high-speed-police-chase)). And another $29.1 million in settlements tied to corrupt Detective Reynaldo Guevara is heading to the council floor ([City Hall faces another $29.1 million in settlements](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/09/det-reynaldo-guevara-settlements-chicago-police-department-misconduct)). Police misconduct payouts remain a chronic drain.

**Key question:** Can the council's alternative revenue ideas actually close the gap, or will a mid-year shortfall force painful cuts? And what happens to the city's bond rating if the pension payment situation recurs?

### 2. Immigration Enforcement and the Sanctuary City Fight

Federal immigration actions saturate this coverage. ICE's Operation Midway Blitz swept through Chicago neighborhoods, rattling communities in Little Village, Pilsen, and Brighton Park ([Chicago after the blitz](https://chicago.suntimes.com/morning-edition/2026/02/23/operation-midway-blitz-trump-jesse-jackson-archdiocese-of-chicago)). Two ICE-linked shootings — Silverio Villegas González killed in Franklin Park and Marimar Martínez wounded in Chicago — prompted Cook County Board President Toni Preckwinkle and a majority of commissioners to demand that State's Attorney Burke investigate ([Preckwinkle calls on top Cook County prosecutor to investigate ICE](https://chicago.suntimes.com/immigration/2026/02/12/preckwinkle-calls-on-top-cook-county-prosecutor-to-investigate-ice-feds-involved-in-chicago-area-shootings)). Burke said her office lacks jurisdiction over federal agents, then later developed a protocol with AG Kwame Raoul to potentially bring charges ([Fiscal principal del Condado de Cook presenta plan](https://chicago.suntimes.com/la-voz/2026/02/20/fiscal-principal-del-condado-de-cook-presenta-plan-para-acusar-a-agentes-de-ice-de-delitos)).

Clergy-led protests at the Broadview ICE facility resulted in 21 arrests; prosecutors dropped charges against 19 ([Cook County prosecutors drop charges against 19 arrested during clergy-led protest](https://chicago.suntimes.com/immigration/2026/02/09/cook-county-prosecutors-drop-charges-against-19-arrested-during-clergy-led-protest-broadview-ice-facility)). Brighton Park businesses took an economic hit from raids, prompting the Illinois Hispanic Chamber of Commerce to organize a buy-local campaign and microgrants ([Campaña de compra local impulsa Brighton Park](https://chicago.suntimes.com/la-voz/2026/02/17/campana-de-compra-local-impulsa-brighton-park-tras-redadas-de-inmigracion-que-perjudicaron-a-negocios-locales)). Attorney Sheryl Weikal petitioned for a special prosecutor to handle alleged ICE misconduct ([How can Chicago's top prosecutor hold ICE accountable?](https://chicago.suntimes.com/public-safety/2026/02/06/how-can-chicagos-top-prosecutor-hold-ice-accountable)).

**Key question:** Will any criminal charge actually be brought against a federal agent? Burke's new protocol creates a framework, but no one has tested it yet. And how long can neighborhoods absorb the economic and psychological toll of enforcement operations?

### 3. Police Accountability and Reform

The Dexter Reed case continues to reverberate. Officer Thomas Spanos, who fired the fatal shots in the 2024 traffic-stop shooting, was accused of threatening a fellow detective at a Norwood Park bar, triggering internal affairs and COPA investigations ([Chicago police officer involved in Dexter Reed shooting accused of threatening detective](https://chicago.suntimes.com/dexter-reed/2026/02/10/chicago-police-dexter-reed-tommys-higgins-norwood-park-fight-copa-traffic-stop)). The Reed family's attorney is pushing COPA to release a report that largely clears the officers involved ([Chicago police watchdog should release report mostly clearing cops in Dexter Reed case](https://chicago.suntimes.com/dexter-reed/2026/02/11/chicago-police-dexter-reed-shooting-killing-copa-cpd-humboldt-park-thomas-spanos)).

Officer Carlos Baker fatally shot his partner, Officer Krystal Rivera, during a June 2025 pursuit — despite Baker's record of failing to report a high-speed chase and inadvertently discharging his Taser. He'd been assigned to the Gresham District tactical team anyway ([Chicago police officer 'inadvertently' fired Taser a year before fatally shooting his partner](https://chicago.suntimes.com/the-watchdogs/2026/02/19/chicago-police-department-krystal-rivera-carlos-baker-gresham-district-misconduct-discipline-investigation-cpd)). The question of how officers with troubled records end up on specialized units is a systemic concern.

A federal jury awarded $5.7 million to the Tate family over a botched 2018 SWAT raid on the South Side ([Jury awards $5.7 million to family targeted in botched police raid](https://chicago.suntimes.com/crime/2026/02/25/chicago-police-wrongful-raid-search-warrant-anjanette-young-tate-family)). And the council is weighing $29.1 million more in Guevara-related settlements ([City Hall faces another $29.1 million in settlements](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/09/det-reynaldo-guevara-settlements-chicago-police-department-misconduct)).

A staffing study by Matrix Consulting Group recommends adding patrol officers and sergeants while shifting 600 sworn officers into civilian roles, giving CPD a data-driven model for resource allocation ([Long-sought Chicago police staffing study recommends adding more cops, hiring civilians](https://chicago.suntimes.com/police-reform/2026/02/12/chicago-police-staffing-hiring-superintendent-larry-snelling-study-matrix-consulting)).

**Key question:** Will the staffing study's recommendations be funded, or will they die in the budget fight? And can COPA's Reed report withstand public scrutiny if it clears officers while Spanos continues to generate misconduct complaints?

### 4. Chicago Public Schools in Crisis

CPS faces a $500 million budget deficit, a superintendent search mired in political tension, and the imminent closure of a charter network.

The Aspira charter schools, serving about 545 high-school students in Avondale, face insolvency and will close, scattering students to new schools after CPS determined further cash advances weren't sustainable ([CPS to shut down financially distressed ASPIRA campuses](https://chicago.suntimes.com/education/2026/02/26/cps-to-shut-down-financially-distressed-aspira-campuses-scattering-students-to-new-schools)). Most board members opposed a mid-year bailout ([CPS charter school faces insolvency by March](https://chicago.suntimes.com/education/2026/02/11/cps-charter-school-cant-make-payroll-insolvent-closed-march-aspira-chiarts-cpsboard)).

The search for a permanent superintendent is stalled — or "on track," depending on which board member you ask. Six elected members say the mayor is interfering; Board President Sean Harden insists the process is fine ([Is CPS superintendent search 'on track' or 'stalled'?](https://chicago.suntimes.com/chicago-school-board/2026/02/13/is-cps-superintendent-search-on-track-or-stalled-depends-who-you-ask-on-school-board)). This fight will intensify as the board transitions to a fully elected body later this year, shifting hiring authority away from the mayor.

CPS is also eyeing changes to the Chicago High School for the Arts that would cut conservatory instruction time to comply with union contract limits, angering parents ([CPS eyes changes to ChiArts conservatory model](https://chicago.suntimes.com/education/2026/02/25/cps-eyes-changes-to-chiarts-conservatory-model-upsetting-parents-who-want-more-arts-during-school-day)). Big money is already flowing into the November school board election, with billionaire Michael Sacks and the SEIU Illinois Council PAC making record donations to incumbents ([Big money expected to flow in November's historic Chicago school board race](https://chicago.suntimes.com/chicago-school-board/2026/02/19/big-money-expected-to-flow-in-novembers-historic-chicago-school-board-race)).

**Key question:** Will the elected board assert real independence from the mayor, or will donor money and political alliances create a new set of dependencies? And who actually lands the permanent superintendent job?

### 5. The Chicago Housing Authority Under Fire

CHA appears in multiple damaging stories. The agency reversed a permanent debarment of Lovie Diggs — the daughter of CHA Board Commissioner Debra Parker — allowing her company to bid on contracts despite a misdemeanor conviction. The CHA Inspector General had flagged the conflict ([Chicago Housing Authority backs off ban on contracts with commissioner's daughter](https://chicago.suntimes.com/2026/02/06/chicago-housing-authority-contracts-commissioner-debra-parker-daughter-lovie-diggs)).

A whistleblower, senior compliance manager Amanda Motyka, alleges CHA has violated federal disability and civil rights laws for years, denying reasonable accommodations to residents. A disabled resident's daughter died while living in an inaccessible unit. HUD is negotiating a voluntary compliance agreement, and CHA paid a $525,000 settlement ([HUD, whistleblower says CHA has violated disability and civil rights laws for years](https://chicago.suntimes.com/the-watchdogs/2026/02/11/cha-hud-federal-disability-civil-rights-laws-ada-whistleblower-complaint)).

The HOPE Fair Housing Center sued CHA and its property managers for letting scattered-site units sit vacant and deteriorate, disproportionately harming Black and Hispanic neighborhoods ([CHA sued by Humboldt Park resident over failing to maintain vacant properties](https://chicago.suntimes.com/the-watchdogs/2026/02/19/cha-sued-humboldt-park-resident-hope-fair-vacant-scattered-site-properties)).

**Key question:** What is HUD's timeline for the compliance agreement, and what enforcement teeth will it have? How many scattered-site units are actually vacant, and what's the deferred maintenance bill?

## Follow the Money

**Police misconduct settlements** remain the city's most expensive open wound. The $29.1 million in Guevara settlements, the rejected $8.25 million police-chase settlement, and the $5.7 million Tate family jury verdict all pull from the same pool of city funds ([City Hall faces another $29.1 million in settlements](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/09/det-reynaldo-guevara-settlements-chicago-police-department-misconduct); [Finance Committee rejects $8.25M settlement](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/11/finance-committee-rejects-8-25m-settlement-to-compensate-family-of-woman-killed-in-high-speed-police-chase); [Jury awards $5.7 million](https://chicago.suntimes.com/crime/2026/02/25/chicago-police-wrongful-raid-search-warrant-anjanette-young-tate-family)). Get a running tab of annual settlement costs; they rival department budgets.

**TIF money** is a constant undercurrent. The city wants $50 million from the Canal/Congress TIF district for the Greyhound station ([Chicago avanza en la obtención de fondos](https://chicago.suntimes.com/la-voz/2026/02/06/chicago-avanza-en-la-obtencion-de-fondos-para-comprar-la-estacion-de-greyhound-del-centro)). A record $1 billion TIF surplus was earmarked for CPS ([Mayoral aides explain Chicago's cash flow crunch](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/09/cash-flow-crunch-partial-pension-payment-mayor-brandon-johnson)). TIF districts fund developments like the Loop's Acoda project, which is eligible for up to $5 million in city grants ([Loop project bringing 300 new apartments](https://chicago.suntimes.com/real-estate/2026/02/18/loop-acoda-project-300-new-apartments-dl3realty-harold-washington-library)). TIF is the city's shadow budget — a reporter needs to know every active district and where the surpluses flow.

**The ticket overcharge ruling** could cost the city up to $138 million in refunds and interest. ## Stories Worth Chasing

**1. CHA's scattered-site vacancy crisis.** Three separate stories — the whistleblower complaint, the HOPE Fair Housing lawsuit, and the Diggs contracting controversy — point to an agency with systemic problems in property management, compliance, and governance. How many scattered-site units are vacant across the city? What is the total deferred maintenance cost? Who are the property management contractors, and how much are they being paid to manage buildings that sit empty? The HUD voluntary compliance agreement should be an obtainable document. ([HUD whistleblower](https://chicago.suntimes.com/the-watchdogs/2026/02/11/cha-hud-federal-disability-civil-rights-laws-ada-whistleblower-complaint); [CHA sued](https://chicago.suntimes.com/the-watchdogs/2026/02/19/cha-sued-humboldt-park-resident-hope-fair-vacant-scattered-site-properties); [CHA backs off ban on contracts](https://chicago.suntimes.com/2026/02/06/chicago-housing-authority-contracts-commissioner-debra-parker-daughter-lovie-diggs))

**2. How dangerous officers end up on tactical teams.** Officer Carlos Baker's path from unreported chases and accidental Taser discharges to the Gresham District tactical team — where he killed his partner — demands a systemic look. Who approves tactical team assignments? What role did Chief of Patrol Jon Hein and Commander Michel Tate play? Are there other officers with red-flag disciplinary records serving on tactical teams now? COPA and Bureau of Internal Affairs files would be the starting point. ([Chicago police officer 'inadvertently' fired Taser](https://chicago.suntimes.com/the-watchdogs/2026/02/19/chicago-police-department-krystal-rivera-carlos-baker-gresham-district-misconduct-discipline-investigation-cpd))

**3. The true cost of the ticket overcharge system.** A judge found the city violated a $250 state fine cap, generating $163 million in illegal penalties. Who designed this fine structure? When was it adopted? Did the Law Department ever flag the cap? How many low-income drivers lost their cars or had wages garnished? The city's response to the ruling — whether it appeals or negotiates — has immediate budget implications. Cross-reference with the driver's license reinstatement story, which showed how suspensions trap low-income residents in poverty ([City sticker violations overcharges](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/24/chicago-overcharge-city-sticker-violations-parking-tickets-fine-cap-cook-county-ruling-refunds); [Teams of lawyers put drivers back on the road](https://chicago.suntimes.com/transportation/2026/02/07/drivers-license-reinstatement-expo-courts-transportation)).

**4. Money and influence in the first fully elected school board race.** Billionaire Michael Sacks, the SEIU PAC, and Sam and Jessica Yagan are already spending on incumbents. Who are these donors, what do they want from CPS, and how do their interests align with the mayor's? The board will control hiring of a permanent superintendent and oversee a district with a $500 million deficit. Map the donor networks now, before November spending explodes. ([Big money expected to flow](https://chicago.suntimes.com/chicago-school-board/2026/02/19/big-money-expected-to-flow-in-novembers-historic-chicago-school-board-race))

**5. The immigration enforcement economic toll.** Brighton Park businesses were hurt badly enough by raids to require a buy-local campaign and microgrants ([Campaña de compra local impulsa Brighton Park](https://chicago.suntimes.com/la-voz/2026/02/17/campana-de-compra-local-impulsa-brighton-park-tras-redadas-de-inmigracion-que-perjudicaron-a-negocios-locales)). After-school programs lost federal funding ([Programas después de la escuela](https://chicago.suntimes.com/la-voz/2026/02/17/programas-despues-de-la-escuela-estan-en-el-limbo-debido-a-recortes-de-la-administracion-de-trump)). HIV prevention grants were slashed ([Trump administration cutting millions in Illinois HIV prevention grants](https://chicago.suntimes.com/donald-trump/2026/02/10/trump-administration-cuts-illinois-hiv-prevention-grants)). No one has assembled a comprehensive accounting of the dollar amount of federal funding at risk in Chicago and which services disappear first. That story would give readers — and city officials — a real picture of what's coming.

**6. The Inspector General vacancy.** Deborah Witzburg is leaving. Her 311 audit showed the kind of work that office can do. Who Johnson appoints — and how independent that person proves to be — will determine whether anyone is watching city operations from the inside. This is a quiet story with enormous consequences. ([Chicago's 311 system turns into a 'black hole'](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/26/chicago-311-system-poor-response-city-hall-inspector-general-deborah-witzburg))

## Quick Reference: Who Does What

**Chicago City Council** — The 50-member legislative body that approves the city budget, confirms mayoral appointments, votes on zoning changes, and authorizes legal settlements. The council majority has broken with Johnson on budget strategy.

**Cook County State's Attorney's Office** — Eileen O'Neill Burke's office prosecutes felonies in Cook County and has become the focal point for whether local charges can be brought against federal immigration agents.

**Cook County Board of Commissioners** — Governs the county's $10 billion budget, including the jail, court system, public health network, and forest preserves. Toni Preckwinkle chairs the board and faces a primary challenge.

**Cook County Board of Review** — Three commissioners who decide property tax assessment appeals. Races for two seats in the March 17 primary will shape how tax burdens are distributed between homeowners and commercial property owners. ([Two Cook County property tax appeal commissioners face strong challengers](https://chicago.suntimes.com/elections/2026/02/26/cook-county-board-of-review-march-17-primary-steele-cardenas-nicholson-irizarry))

**Cook County Treasurer's Office** — Maria Pappas's office collects and distributes property taxes. Delayed property tax receipts contributed to the city's cash-flow crunch, and Pappas herself faces ethics complaints over alleged bullying of Tyler Technologies executives during a tax-system overhaul.

**Chicago Board of Education** — Oversees CPS, which enrolls roughly 320,000 students. Currently a hybrid of mayoral appointees and elected members transitioning to a fully elected body. Controls the superintendent search and a budget with a $500 million deficit.

**Chicago Housing Authority (CHA)** — Manages public housing, including scattered-site units and Housing Choice Vouchers. Currently facing a HUD compliance negotiation, a federal lawsuit over vacant properties, and Inspector General scrutiny of contracting practices.

**Civilian Office of Police Accountability (COPA)** — The civilian body that investigates police misconduct complaints, including use-of-force cases. Its unreleased report on the Dexter Reed shooting is a live issue.

**Chicago Department of Planning and Development (DPD)** — Oversees zoning, TIF districts, and development proposals. Commissioner Ciere Boatright steers projects from the Plan Commission to the City Council.

**Community Development Commission** — Reviews and recommends TIF expenditures, including the proposed $50 million Greyhound station purchase.

**Finance Committee (City Council)** — Chaired by Ald. Pat Dowell (3rd Ward). Approves legal settlements, including police misconduct payouts. Its rejection of the $8.25 million police-chase settlement shows the committee is willing to push back.

**Illinois Gaming Board** — State body that licenses gambling operations. Beale's push to force video gambling applications runs through this board.

**Chicago Department of Business Affairs and Consumer Protection** — Licenses businesses, enforces regulations, and recently imposed steep fee increases. Commissioner Ivan Capifali is also handling the taxi fare-hike proposal and the sex-dungeon cease-and-desist.

**City Inspector General** — An independent watchdog that audits city operations. Outgoing IG Deborah Witzburg's successor will be a Johnson appointment. The office's independence is not guaranteed.

**Office of Emergency Management and Communications (OEMC)** — Runs the 311 system for nonemergency complaints and the 911 dispatch center. The 311 audit found systemic failures in responding to and tracking resident requests.
