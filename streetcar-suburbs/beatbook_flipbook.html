Chapter 1
Executive Snapshot
3

The College Park City Council operates at the precise intersection where a university town must reckon with its past while building its future — a tension that runs through nearly every vote, every public comment period, every debate over who gets to live here and what it will cost them. Mayor Fazlul Kabir, a fixture in city politics who served six terms on council before becoming mayor, presides over a government wrestling with affordability in all its forms: the University of Maryland students cornered into re-signing leases four weeks after moving in, the seniors who need housing they can afford on 60% of area median income, the displaced residents of Lakeland who watched urban renewal bulldoze their vibrant neighborhood between 1969 and 1981 and now, through the Restorative Justice Commission chaired by Maxine Gross, are asking for something the city has never given — reparations in the form of money, housing priority, and free college tuition for those who can prove they lived there at least ten years. These aren't abstract policy questions. They are fights about who belongs in College Park and what the city owes to those it pushed out or priced out.

4

The council itself has grown younger and more diverse in recent elections, bringing in members like Jacob Hernandez and Alan Hew alongside newcomers Daniel Oates, Kelly Jordan, and Holly Simmons — Jordan winning her District 2 seat by just three votes after a hand recount. They debate everything from whether to double their own $10,000 annual salaries to whether 16-year-olds should vote in city elections (the council voted 5-3 to lower the voting age, making College Park the ninth Maryland city to do so). They pressure developers like Article Student Living to include affordable units in a proposed 192-unit complex on Guilford Drive, with Denise Mitchell declaring, "We have a vision moving forward, and we're not going to negate that vision." They pause membership in the Prince George's County Municipal Association after a former treasurer allegedly embezzled over $30,000, with Hernandez asking bluntly, "Why would we give money to an organization that is clearly lacking integrity?" Power here is exercised in ordinances that fine landlords $500 for pressuring early lease renewals, in letters sent to the Prince George's County Board of Education defending the Chinese immersion program at Paint Branch Elementary, in decisions about whether to annex the soon-to-be-vacant Beltsville Agricultural Research Center or build a $2 million bridge connecting Old Calvert Road Park to the Northeast Branch Trail — a project the community has requested for at least a decade.

5

This archive captures a city in continuous negotiation with itself — over Route 1 and Baltimore Avenue development, over student housing and neighborhood tensions, over public safety strategies that don't involve creating a city police force (Hew, who has graduated from three citizen police academies, brings unusual expertise to these conversations). The council navigates relationships with powerful neighbors: the University of Maryland, Prince George's County, the Maryland-National Capital Park and Planning Commission. They celebrate Helen Reniere turning 100 at Attick Towers and Hollywood Elementary students getting new shoes from Coca-Cola's bottler, then turn around and increase fines for code violations to align with state law allowing municipalities to levy penalties up to $5,000. The stakes are local but the questions are universal: How does a city grow without displacing the people who built it? How does it house students without turning established neighborhoods into rental markets? How does it repair harm done generations ago while managing the immediate crisis of child care costs and traffic noise on Edmonston Road? These stories span recent years of council action, a period marked by pandemic recovery, housing shortages, and the election of Joseline Peña-Melnyk — a former College Park councilmember — as the 109th Speaker of the Maryland House of Delegates, a reminder that local politics here is a training ground for power that reaches far beyond city limits.

Top Recurring Issues
7
Affordable Housing and Development Pressure

College Park sits at a crossroads: it wants to grow, but not at the cost of pricing people out. The tension plays out in council chambers almost weekly. When Article Student Living pitched a 192-unit complex on Guilford Drive, Mayor Fazlul Kabir pushed them toward the city's tax incentive program — "The catch is that you must have affordable housing in it," he said. Councilmember Denise Mitchell made it plain: "We have a vision moving forward, and we're not going to negate that vision."

That vision collides with market forces. A four-story, 87-unit senior housing project on Branchville Road offered a rare win — affordable units pegged to 60% of area median income, financed through federal low-income housing tax credits. Councilmember Kelly Jordan called it out bluntly: "There is a real need for senior housing in College Park." But when Maryland Governor Wes Moore proposed allowing eight houses per acre statewide, State Sen. Jim Rosapepe and County Councilmember Tom Dernoga pushed back, citing congestion and public safety worries. House Speaker Joseline Peña-Melnyk countered with numbers: Maryland needs 100,000 more homes. "We need more housing choices. Smaller homes. Townhouses. Accessory dwelling units."

The city runs its own programs — the College Park Community Preservation Trust sells single-family homes to eligible buyers, while Live+Work College Park offers zero-interest loans to full-time city employees buying in. But the pressure doesn't ease. Students complain about "studentification" driving up rents. Homeowners watch owner-occupied houses flip into rentals. Christopher Gill, running for council, framed it as an identity crisis: "I don't want people to be pushed out of their community."

Student Tenant Protections
8

Nearly 30 people, mostly students, packed a council meeting to demand protection from landlords pressuring them to renew leases before they'd unpacked their moving boxes. Nisha Kumar captured the bind: "We've just moved in for the semester and we already have to figure out next year... Who's going to take my spot? ... Will I have to pay over $1,000 a month for a place I'm not even living in?" Scott Tebbe said it plainer: "This was a decision I wasn't ready to make, and I felt trapped."

The council responded. They passed an ordinance — unanimously — preventing landlords from asking tenants to renew sooner than 180 days before lease end. Sami Saeed, a public policy major, had told them about getting hit with a steep rent increase just four weeks after moving in: "The stress that that caused me … trying to find anyone to live with for next year. I had to call 50 people." First offense: $500 fine. Repeat violations: $2,500.

Landlords opposed it, arguing it limited their control over tenant selection. But the students kept coming to meetings. Frances Chen, Nicholas Crane, and Ashna Pandya presented research on neighborhood tensions, arguing that both the city and university needed to do more. "We found that the most successful communities have used deep university and city cooperation to create a solution that works for both students and [homeowners]," Chen said. Councilmember Alan Hew agreed the university should step up: they needed to teach students how to be good neighbors.

Reparations and Restorative Justice for Lakeland

Maxine Gross has been at this for years. Urban renewal swept through Lakeland between 1969 and 1981, displacing families who'd built a community. Now Gross chairs the 20-member Restorative Justice Commission, and she refuses to let the city forget. "When you talk to people who experienced Lakeland before urban renewal, they speak of a vibrant community."

9

The commission delivered recommendations: financial compensation, housing priority, free college tuition for anyone who lived in Lakeland at least 10 years or their direct descendants. It's not symbolic. It's money, access, acknowledgment. The council agreed to discuss it in future meetings, but those discussions haven't moved fast. Meanwhile, the city partnered with University of Maryland to add 10 murals highlighting Lakeland's history — $28,382 total budget. Gross called it "a way for folks to be able to see the community that we have been talking about for a number of years." Mayor Kabir said it would be "a wonderful and very easy and entertaining way to know about what happened."

The mural project is one thing. Reparations are another. When District 2 candidates Holly Simmons, Kelly Jordan, and Chris Gill faced off at a forum, they all agreed restorative justice for Lakeland was critical. Simmons said reparations were "very important." Jordan suggested new developments should include affordable units. But commitment in a forum is different from votes in chambers. The commission waits.

Public Safety Without a City Police Force

College Park doesn't have its own police department. That reality shapes every conversation about safety. District 3 candidates Dan Oates and Ray Ranker debated alternatives during their race. Oates pushed back against the obvious ask: "I think jumping to the answer of 'we need a police force' is premature." Ranker suggested security cameras, blue light phones, practical measures that don't require hiring and managing sworn officers.

Councilmember Alan Hew took a different approach. He graduated from three citizen police academies — Prince George's County Police, University of Maryland Police, the Sheriff's Office. "There's a lot of speculation of how they operate and I wanted to be able to speak confidently in support of the departments that operate within our city," he said. His training lets him translate between residents and the county and university departments that actually patrol College Park. He can answer questions, explain procedures, build trust.

10

But the underlying tension persists: the city relies on outside departments with their own priorities. Parking Enforcement Officer Jim Miller proposed expanding permit parking to manage congestion — "It's a matter of how that restricted parking is utilized" — but parking enforcement isn't the same as violent crime response. Former Councilmember Susan Whitney suggested incentives like tax breaks to get people to own fewer cars, connecting parking to broader sustainability goals. The city threads a needle, trying to improve safety without the budget or authority for a force of its own.

Child Care Costs and Availability

The Child Care Task Force, appointed by council, delivered nearly 20 recommendations aimed at lowering costs and increasing availability. The city has 18 licensed providers: 11 day care centers, five home-based operations, two tax-exempt religious organizations offering after-school care. It's not enough. Gavin Neubauer, a University of Maryland public policy student on the task force, put it in economic development terms: "If you're able to even increase the amount of businesses here that can survive and care for children, we're going to be a real standout city in this entire metropolitan area."

The proposals include grants to home-based providers and property tax credits for established centers — tools to keep existing operations viable and attract new ones. For families trying to balance work and raising kids in College Park, child care costs can force impossible choices. The task force treated it as infrastructure: if families can't afford care, they can't stay. If they can't stay, the city loses the households it claims to want.

Voting Age and Civic Participation

The council voted 5-3 to drop the voting age for city elections from 18 to 16. Lucas Rigg, 16, said it gave people who didn't choose to live in College Park a say in how it's run. Lovisa Jordan, also 16, called it cool — "I think it's an option that we can get our voices and opinions heard." She thought preparing to vote might make teenagers more aware of city issues.

11

It made College Park the ninth Maryland city to allow teen voting. Six 16- and 17-year-olds cast ballots in the November election — the first time that age group could participate. Turnout overall was 9.9%: 1,598 people out of registered voters. The teen votes were symbolic, a signal about who the city considers stakeholders. But symbols matter in a city where students already feel squeezed by landlords and homeowners feel overrun by renters. Expanding the franchise was a bet on engagement, on the idea that people who have a vote will care about the outcome.

School Quality and Educational Partnerships

When the Prince George's County Board of Education threatened the Chinese immersion program at Paint Branch Elementary, the council sent a letter. The program had become part of College Park's broader educational draw, an amenity for families choosing to settle here. Paint Branch PTA Secretary Alexandra Tyukavina proposed an alternative: add an opt-out option and a full-day English-speaking teacher for families who didn't want Mandarin lessons. "We, the Paint Branch school community, have found that the board's proposal to eliminate the program is not the only way to address the budget issues, nor is it ideal for the students and families within the school."

Meanwhile, a citizen-led committee kept pushing the council on school bus stop safety. Donald Hays, the chairman, resubmitted a report recommending upgrades at seven stops that scored low on evaluations. His frustration was palpable: "I personally feel that is an outrage and there has been very little movement and very little progress." The recommendations — bike lanes, sidewalks — were basic infrastructure, not rocket science. But movement was slow.

12

At Hollywood Elementary, Coca-Cola's local bottler gave all 301 students new sneakers, socks, and school supplies through the Back-to-School with Purpose initiative. Principal Carlos Johnson said, "When you can provide for a parent and the things that they need at home, school and home become one." Councilmember Jacob Hernandez praised Johnson's outreach: "He's gone above and beyond to make sure he's providing meaningful outreach." The event showed community support, but also underlined need — when corporate giveaways make news, it's because families can't cover basics on their own.

Economic Development Along Route 1 and Baltimore Avenue

Route 1 and Baltimore Avenue form College Park's commercial spine. When Wonder opened a food hall offering 15-plus restaurant brands, Councilmember John Rigg said the city had worked hard to bring an exciting concept. "I think they hit it out of the park." Mayor Kabir called it a milestone. Economic Development Director Michael Williams pointed to location: "the epicenter of downtown College Park," perfect for variety.

The city chases that kind of win because it needs the tax base and the vibrancy. But development is uneven. Mayor Kabir signed an ordinance requiring owners of vacant homes and commercial property to register with the city — $25 fee, fines for non-compliance. "Vacant properties can negatively impact our community... When left unmonitored, they can become safety hazards, attract vandalism and contribute to blight," he said. Councilmember Hernandez backed it: "It supports improving public safety... It protects property values."

The city also eyes annexation. Hernandez said they're in early stages of talking about annexing the soon-to-be-vacant Beltsville Agricultural Research Center and other properties. "Should a property fall within the city limits, the residents of College Park have much more of a say in what gets built there," he said. It's a land grab with a community benefit pitch: annex to protect, to control, to grow the tax base. Economic development isn't just about attracting businesses — it's about controlling the landscape before someone else does.

Sustainability and Micromobility
13

Electric scooters and bikes from Veo saw 62% ridership growth between fall 2024 and fall 2025. Mayor Kabir loved it: "We are very happy that we have this micromobility system in College Park because this is in line with our strategic sustainability goal. Every time someone rides a Veo scooter, that person is not using the car, right? So we're taking out the cars from the street."

But the scooters cluttered sidewalks and doorways. Veo implemented stricter parking rules — $4 to $5 extra for parking outside designated areas, fines up to $60 for repeat offenders. Alex Posorske, Veo's East Coast Policy and Partnerships Manager, framed it as enforcement for the common good. The city wants sustainability but also order, a tension that plays out in bike lanes, tree plantings, and every discussion about whether to widen roads or narrow them.

A city-commissioned noise analysis on Edmonston Road found traffic "clearly audible in the front yards," loud enough to warrant more study and potential mitigation by the State Highway Administration. Residents like Maria Cordone said noise spiked after the city removed trees between Edmonston Road and Kenilworth Avenue. Councilmember Ray Ranker said the city was preparing to plant trees and shrubs to help with sound and beautification. Some residents wanted traffic-calming devices to stop speeders. It's the same sustainability question from another angle: how do you balance car throughput with livability?

When Trump administration cuts threatened local residents, Kabir increased the city's financial assistance budget to $100,000. He pushed Article Student Living to include affordable units in their Guilford Drive proposal and reminded residents during Winter Storm Fern to report icy sidewalks through the city's interactive map. Institutions
22
College Park City Council makes the core decisions shaping the city's future, from lowering the voting age to 16 to requiring landlords to wait 180 days before asking tenants to renew leases. The eight-member body has skewed younger with newcomers like Daniel Oates, Kelly Jordan, and Holly Simmons joining. They've pushed developers on affordable housing requirements, paused membership in the county municipal association over embezzlement concerns, endorsed the Old Calvert Road bridge, and doubled down on restorative justice for Lakeland residents. The council debates everything from Veo scooter parking rules to whether paying councilmembers $20,000 instead of $10,000 would attract better candidates. They're wrestling with annexation questions, vacant property registries, noise ordinance enforcement, and how to support residents hit by federal job cuts. University of Maryland dominates the physical and economic landscape, bringing thousands of students who reshape neighborhoods through "studentification" — high rents, tenant turnover, and tensions with long-term residents. Three students presented research to the council showing that successful communities use "deep university and city cooperation" to balance affordable housing for students with code enforcement for homeowners. The university partners with the city on projects like the Lakeland heritage murals, but councilmembers like Alan Hew argue the institution should teach undergraduates "how to be good neighbors" rather than leaving problems for the city to solve. The University of Maryland Police Department runs its own citizen academy, and the school's influence extends to Paint Branch Elementary's Chinese immersion program, which draws College Park families and prompted council support when budget cuts threatened it.
23
Prince George's County sets the broader policy context for College Park, from school board decisions on the Paint Branch immersion program to State Highway Administration noise studies on Kenilworth Avenue traffic. County Councilmember Tom Dernoga opposed Governor Moore's housing density bill over concerns about congestion and pollution, showing county-level resistance to state mandates. The county police department patrols College Park, and Police Chief operations affect local safety discussions. County Executive Aisha Braveboy mobilizes snow crews during storms. The Maryland-National Capital Park and Planning Commission shapes zoning and development decisions. College Park exists in this county web, sometimes aligning, sometimes pushing back, always negotiating between county authority and city priorities. Prince George's County Municipal Association faced crisis when a former treasurer allegedly embezzled over $30,000, prompting College Park to pause membership. The association allows small cities to "stand together and speak with one voice," as President Edouard Haba put it, but the theft exposed governance failures. Haba promised financial oversight committees and reviews, hoping to restore trust. The association represents College Park's relationship with peer municipalities — Laurel, Hyattsville, and others navigating similar development pressures and county dynamics. Whether College Park rejoins depends on the association proving it can manage taxpayer funds responsibly.

Prince George's County Board of Education controls school policy that affects College Park families, particularly the Chinese immersion program at Paint Branch Elementary. The board's proposal to eliminate the program to address budget issues drew council opposition and prompted PTA Secretary Alexandra Tyukavina to propose an opt-out alternative with a full-day English-speaking teacher. The council sent a letter defending years of investment by "educators, students and the community." Schools like Hollywood Elementary, where Principal Carlos Johnson builds community partnerships and secured Coca-Cola's donation of shoes and supplies for all 301 students, depend on board funding and policy decisions that ripple through College Park neighborhoods.

Route 1 runs through College Park like a spine, and nearly every major economic development decision, transit conversation, or fight over student housing touches it in some way. The corridor has drawn attention for new commercial projects — Wonder's food hall landing on Baltimore Avenue marked a moment city leaders framed as validation of years of redevelopment work. But Route 1 also surfaces in discussions about traffic flow, pedestrian safety, and the city's efforts to reshape its downtown identity. When councilmembers talk about making College Park a destination rather than a pass-through, they're talking about Route 1.

Lakeland carries weight beyond its physical footprint. The neighborhood's history — displacement through urban renewal between 1969 and 1981 — has become a central thread in the city's reckoning with restorative justice. The Restorative Justice Commission centered its work on former Lakeland residents, recommending financial compensation, housing priority, and free college tuition for those who lived there at least a decade or their descendants. Maxine Gross, who chairs the commission, described a "vibrant community" lost to renewal. Now the neighborhood is adding ten murals to preserve its heritage, part of a decades-long effort to keep memory visible. When the city approved funding for that project — $28,382 total — it was framing preservation as a form of accountability. A reporter covering equity or housing policy in College Park will find Lakeland central to those conversations.

Edmonston Road has become a focal point for noise complaints and traffic mitigation debates. Ray Ranker said the city planned to plant trees and shrubs between the streets to help with sound and beautification, though some residents wanted the State Highway Administration to install traffic-calming devices to stop speeders. The street illustrates how infrastructure decisions ripple outward — cut down trees for one project, create a noise problem that requires a new fix.

North College Park, Calvert Hills, and Berwyn surface as established residential neighborhoods where tensions around student housing and "studentification" play out. Alan Hew emphasized that the university should teach students how to be good neighbors. These neighborhoods represent the front line of debates about preserving residential character while accommodating a large student population.

Old Town doesn't dominate the archive, but it appears in contexts suggesting it's a distinct area of the city with its own identity. A reporter should track how Old Town fits into broader conversations about neighborhood preservation and whether it faces similar pressures as other residential zones.

Chapter 7
Timeline and Trend Shifts
33

The College Park beat has moved through several distinct phases, each leaving visible marks on how the city governs and what demands attention at the dais.

The voting age debate resolved in 2025 when the council voted 5-3 to let 16- and 17-year-olds cast ballots in city elections. Lucas Rigg and Lovisa Jordan, both 16 at the time, spoke to what it meant — getting voices heard, preparing for civic life. Six teenagers voted in the November election that followed. The question shifted from whether young people should vote to how their participation would shape future campaigns. College Park became the ninth Maryland city to lower the voting age, but the real story now is what those young voters will demand from candidates who suddenly need to speak to them.

The council itself has turned over significantly. Llatetra Brown Esters and Susan Whitney left their District 2 seats, replaced after a tight race by Holly Simmons and Kelly Jordan — Jordan won by three votes after a hand recount against Chris Gill. Daniel Oates took District 3 unopposed. The generation shift was real: younger members brought different questions about utility bills, school quality, parks. Daniel Oates noted it wasn't just age but new perspectives. The old rhythm of council discussions changed. Newer members asked more questions, pushed familiar topics in unfamiliar directions.

Fazlul Kabir's long tenure as mayor — he served six prior council terms before becoming mayor — provides continuity, but even his priorities have shifted with the city's evolution. Early focus on strengthening finances and opening community spaces has given way to urgent conversations about housing affordability, restorative justice for Lakeland, and managing growth pressures from the University of Maryland.

34

The Lakeland restorative justice question moved from background concern to active demand. The 20-member Restorative Justice Commission, chaired by Maxine Gross, delivered specific recommendations: financial compensation, housing priority, free college tuition for those who lived in Lakeland at least 10 years or their direct descendants. Urban renewal between 1969 and 1981 displaced a vibrant community — Gross's words about people remembering Lakeland before carry weight. Ten murals now highlight the neighborhood's history, funded at $28,382 through city, community, and University of Maryland collaboration. The question is no longer whether reparations matter but what form they take and when the council acts. This beat now requires understanding what Lakeland was, what urban renewal did, and how long residents have waited for acknowledgment.

Housing affordability threads through nearly every major decision. The council adopted an ordinance preventing landlords from asking tenants to renew leases sooner than 180 days from the end of the current lease. Sami Saeed, a public policy student, told the story of being approached four weeks after moving in with a substantial rent increase, calling 50 people trying to find next-year housing. Nearly 30 people, mostly student tenants, filled a council meeting demanding protection from early lease pressure. Nisha Kumar and Scott Tebbe described feeling trapped — moving in for the semester, already figuring out next year, wondering if they'd pay over $1,000 monthly for units they wouldn't occupy. The ordinance passed unanimously, fining landlords $500 for first offenses, $2,500 for repeats.

35

But the bigger housing fight involves supply and affordability at scale. Governor Wes Moore pushed legislation allowing developers to build at least eight houses per acre. House Speaker Joseline Peña-Melnyk, who represents College Park and is a former councilmember now serving as Maryland's 109th House Speaker, strongly advocated for the proposal, citing a 100,000-home shortage statewide. State Sen. Jim Rosapepe and County Councilmember Tom Dernoga pushed back over congestion, pollution, public safety. Local debates about student housing on Guilford Drive — a 192-unit Article Student Living complex replacing Hope Lutheran Church — saw Mayor Kabir and Councilmember Denise Mitchell press for affordable units, with Kabir encouraging use of the city's tax incentive program: "The catch is that you must have affordable housing in it." Mitchell emphasized the city won't negate its vision. Pastor Julie Bringman noted the new church space would be larger, fully accessible, possibly with room for affordable units.

Branchville Crossing, an 87-unit senior building on Branchville Road for residents 62 and older, secured council support after addressing parking ordinances. Rent ties to 60 percent of area median income, financed through 4 percent federal low-income housing tax credits. Councilmember Kelly Jordan stated the real need for senior housing. The Flats at College Park offers 317 units with income-restricted options. The College Park Community Preservation Trust sells single-family homes to eligible buyers. Live+Work College Park provides zero-interest loans to full-time city employees buying homes locally. The Child Care Task Force, including University of Maryland student Gavin Neubauer, proposed grants to home-based providers and property tax credits to established centers, aiming to make College Park "a real standout city" in affordability. But housing remains pressure, not resolution. Chris Gill's concern about owner-occupied homes flipping to rentals, people being pushed out — that's the beat's persistent undertow.

36

Student-neighborhood tensions have evolved from background friction to structured problem-solving. Frances Chen, Nicholas Crane, and Ashna Pandya presented to council on "studentification" of established neighborhoods, high rents, the need for university-city cooperation. Chen noted successful communities balance affordable student housing with code enforcement. Councilmember Alan Hew emphasized the university should teach students to be good neighbors — a pointed assignment of responsibility. Permit parking expansions remain under consideration. Parking Enforcement Officer Jim Miller proposed studies on restricting additional streets. Former Councilmember Susan Whitney suggested tax breaks to encourage residents to own fewer cars, framing it as incentivizing rather than punishing. These aren't abstract planning debates — they're about who gets to live where, who parks where, who feels squeezed.

Public safety discussions have matured without producing a city police force. Alan Hew graduated from three citizen police academies — Prince George's County Police, University of Maryland Police, Prince George's County Sheriff. He wanted to speak confidently supporting departments operating in the city, building trust, sharing insights. District 3 candidates Dan Oates and Ray Ranker discussed improving safety without creating municipal police, suggesting security cameras, blue light phones. The beat requires knowing what Prince George's County Police provides, what UMD Police covers, where gaps exist, what residents actually experience versus what officials claim works.

37

Infrastructure projects have long timelines and visible frustration. The Old Calvert Road bridge connecting the park to Northeast Branch Trail has been requested for at least 10 years. Estimated cost: $2 million. Ray Ranker stated the community didn't want to wait another month — they wanted construction started tomorrow. The council endorsed it. Whether it gets built is the next chapter. Donald Hays's school bus stop safety subcommittee resubmitted recommendations for seven low-scoring stops, urging bike lanes and sidewalks. Hays called the lack of action "an outrage," noting very little movement. These delays accumulate into resident skepticism about whether the city delivers.

Economic development brings visible wins and complicated questions. Wonder food hall opened on Baltimore Avenue, its first Maryland location, with over 15 restaurants and brands. John Rigg said the city worked hard to bring an exciting concept, and they hit it out of the park. Kabir called it a milestone. Economic Development Director Michael Williams noted the epicenter location downtown. Route 1 and Baltimore Avenue remain focal points for development activity. Paint Branch Elementary's Chinese immersion program drew council support when the county school board threatened it. Paint Branch PTA Secretary Alexandra Tyukavina proposed alternatives to budget cuts. The program represents years of investment by educators, students, community — cutting it undermines that ecosystem.

38

Environmental sustainability threads through policy but hasn't dominated headlines. Kabir praised Veo's micromobility system — every scooter ride removes a car from streets, aligning with strategic sustainability goals. Veo usage increased 62 percent between fall 2024 and fall 2025, prompting stricter parking rules: $4 to $5 charges for parking outside designated areas, $60 fines for repeated violations. Alex Posorske, Veo's East Coast Policy and Partnerships Manager, defended the changes. The noise analysis on Edmonston Road by RK&K found traffic "clearly audible in front yards," warranting detailed study and possible State Highway Administration mitigation. Residents like Maria Cordone complained noise increased after the city removed trees between Edmonston Road and Kenilworth Avenue. Ray Ranker said the city would plant trees and shrubs for sound mitigation and beautification, though residents wanted traffic-calming devices for speeders. John Rigg's expertise as a volunteer paramedic with Calvert Advanced Life Support influenced his opposition to speed humps on Autoville Drive — long bone fractures like hips or pelvises hurt over bumps. He suggested sidewalks instead.

Annexation discussions are in initial stages but signal territorial ambitions. The soon-to-be-vacant Beltsville Agricultural Research Center and nearby properties are under consideration. Jacob Hernandez noted the city would consider annexing for protection, economic development, or population boost. If property falls within city limits, College Park residents have much more say in what gets built. This is early, but it's a beat-shaping question: how large does College Park want to become, and what fights will annexation trigger?

39

Governance questions keep surfacing. The council considered doubling member pay from $10,000 to $20,000 annually. Hernandez said everyone works incredibly hard, and the increase might not be extraordinarily significant but helps cover costs. Ray Ranker agreed, noting if pay were $1 million, more people would run than if it were $0. The proposal aims to make positions attractive to more community members. The council paused membership in the Prince George's County Municipal Association after a former treasurer allegedly embezzled over $30,000. Hernandez asked why the city would give money to an organization clearly lacking integrity. PGCMA President Edouard Haba promised financial reviews and oversight committees, hoping towns are stronger when they stand together and speak with one voice. Whether College Park rejoins is unresolved.

The council introduced an ordinance increasing fines for city code violations — construction, fire safety, housing, noise, unruly social gatherings — up to $5,000, aligning with Maryland law. Director of Public Services Jatinder Khokhar and City Attorney Stephanie Anderson presented it. John Rigg noted unruly social gatherings almost always include noise and much more. The vacant property registration ordinance requires owners to register empty buildings for $25, facing fines for non-compliance. Kabir said vacant properties negatively impact the community — safety hazards, vandalism, blight. Hernandez agreed it supports public safety and protects property values.

40

National politics landed hard during President Donald Trump's second term. Funding cuts, employee layoffs, executive orders hit the city. Kabir said the city doesn't know how things will actually go in the future but will continue supporting the community. The financial assistance program budget increased to $100,000 to help residents affected by job losses, including those from the Beltsville Agricultural Research Center and the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration. Winter storm Fern brought snow, sleet, freezing rain to the Route 1 corridor. Prince George's County Executive Aisha Braveboy said crews worked around the clock. Kabir reminded residents to report icy sidewalks and check when streets were last plowed via an interactive city map.

Community celebrations provide texture. Helen Reniere turned 100 at Attick Towers, telling the council she gets up in the morning, takes her medicine, puts Jesus first. She praised her friendship with Charlotte Davis, a staff member. Queens Chapel Barbershop celebrated 85 years with a party attended by Hyattsville officials and customers. Merrill Hartson, a retired AP News editor, gave poetical remarks comparing the shop to Cheers — everyone knows your name. Hollywood Elementary's 301 students got new sneakers, socks, school supplies from the local Coca-Cola bottler through Back-to-School with Purpose. Principal Carlos Johnson said providing for parents makes school and home one. Jacob Hernandez praised Johnson's meaningful outreach and relationship-building.

The persistent throughlines are housing pressure, student-resident friction, slow infrastructure delivery, and justice deferred for Lakeland. What's rising is impatience — younger council members asking harder questions, residents demanding action not studies, specific policy proposals replacing vague promises. What's fading is tolerance for delay and deflection. The beat requires tracking whether the restorative justice commission's recommendations become reality, whether housing production matches rhetoric, whether students get protection from exploitative landlords, whether infrastructure projects move from endorsement to completion.

A reporter covering this beat today faces a council with new energy, old problems, and constituents increasingly unwilling to wait another decade for promises to materialize.
