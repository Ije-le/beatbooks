## Summary

College Park is a college town trying to be more than one — more affordable, more equitable, more in control of its own future. Mayor Fazlul Kabir, who served six terms on council before winning the mayor's seat in a special election, presides over a city that's constantly negotiating between competing pressures: university growth and neighborhood stability, economic development and displacement, past injustice and present politics.

The city came to this moment through a jarring transition. Patrick Wojahn, College Park's previous mayor, resigned the night before his arrest on March 2 and was subsequently convicted on 60 counts of distribution of child pornography. He's serving 30 years of a 150-year sentence. Denise Mitchell stepped up as pro tem mayor until Kabir won the special election. That backstory hangs over the current council, which has moved to reestablish trust while also taking on its most ambitious policy agenda in years.

The current council — Kabir as mayor; Jacob Hernandez and Alan Hew representing District 1; Holly Simmons and Kelly Jordan in District 2; Daniel Oates and Ray Ranker in District 3; Maria Mackie and Denise Mitchell in District 4 — is younger and more diverse than councils past. Jordan won her seat by three votes after a hand recount. Oates ran District 3 unopposed. They've passed a tenant protection ordinance stopping landlords from pressuring early lease renewals, pushed developers on affordable housing, voted 5-3 to lower the voting age to 16, and told a developer straight up: "We have a vision moving forward, and we're not going to negate that."

Underneath the day-to-day votes runs a deeper question that the archive keeps circling back to: who does College Park actually belong to? The students who fill its apartments and pack council meetings demanding rent protections? The long-time homeowners in Calvert Hills and Berwyn watching their neighborhoods flip to rentals? The former residents of Lakeland whose community was bulldozed by urban renewal between 1969 and 1981, who are now waiting on reparations the city keeps promising to discuss? That tension doesn't resolve — it just shifts shape depending on which vote is on the docket.

## The Main Issues

**Affordable Housing and Development Pressure**

College Park wants to grow without pricing people out. That's a hard needle to thread, and the council debates it almost every meeting. When Article Student Living pitched a 192-unit complex on Guilford Drive to replace Hope Lutheran Church, Mayor Kabir pushed them toward the city's tax incentive program: "The catch is that you must have affordable housing in it." Denise Mitchell made it plain: "We have a vision moving forward, and we're not going to negate that vision."

A four-story, 87-unit senior building on Branchville Road offered a rare win — rent at 60% of area median income, financed through federal low-income housing tax credits. Kelly Jordan was direct: "There is a real need for senior housing in College Park." But the bigger housing fight plays out at the state level. When Governor Wes Moore pushed a bill allowing eight houses per acre statewide, House Speaker Joseline Peña-Melnyk — a former College Park councilmember — backed it hard, citing a 100,000-home shortage. State Sen. Jim Rosapepe and County Councilmember Tom Dernoga pushed back over congestion worries. The city runs its own programs — the College Park Community Preservation Trust and zero-interest loans through Live+Work College Park — but pressure doesn't ease. As one candidate put it: "I don't want people to be pushed out of their community."

**Student Tenant Protections**

Nearly 30 people, mostly students, packed a council meeting to demand protection from landlords pressuring them to renew leases before they'd unpacked. Nisha Kumar said it plain: "We've just moved in for the semester and we already have to figure out next year... Will I have to pay over $1,000 a month for a place I'm not even living in?" The council responded — unanimously. They passed an ordinance preventing landlords from seeking renewals sooner than 180 days before lease end. First offense: $500. Repeat violations: $2,500.

Landlords opposed it. Students kept coming to meetings. Frances Chen, Nicholas Crane, and Ashna Pandya presented research on neighborhood tensions, arguing both the city and university needed to step up. Councilmember Alan Hew agreed the university should teach students how to be good neighbors.

**Reparations and Restorative Justice for Lakeland**

Maxine Gross has been at this for years. Urban renewal swept through Lakeland between 1969 and 1981, displacing families who'd built a community. Now Gross chairs the 20-member Restorative Justice Commission, and she doesn't let the city forget. "When you talk to people who experienced Lakeland before urban renewal, they speak of a vibrant community."

The commission delivered real recommendations: financial compensation, housing priority, free college tuition for anyone who lived in Lakeland at least 10 years or their direct descendants. The council agreed to discuss it — but those discussions move slowly. Meanwhile, the city funded 10 murals highlighting Lakeland's history ($28,382 total). Gross called it "a way for folks to be able to see the community that we have been talking about for a number of years." The murals are one thing. Reparations are another.

**Public Safety Without a City Police Force**

College Park doesn't have its own police department. That shapes every safety conversation. District 3 candidates Dan Oates and Ray Ranker debated alternatives during their race: cameras, blue light phones, practical measures. Oates was blunt: "I think jumping to the answer of 'we need a police force' is premature."

Alan Hew took a different approach — he graduated from three citizen police academies (Prince George's County Police, University of Maryland Police, and the Sheriff's Office) to be able to speak confidently about how they operate. His training helps him translate between residents and the county and university departments that actually patrol College Park. The underlying tension remains: the city relies on outside agencies with their own priorities.

**Child Care Costs and Availability**

The Child Care Task Force delivered nearly 20 recommendations: grants to home-based providers, property tax credits for established centers. The city has 18 licensed providers — 11 day care centers, five home operations, two tax-exempt religious organizations. It's not enough. Gavin Neubauer, a University of Maryland public policy student on the task force, framed it as economic development: "If you're able to even increase the amount of businesses here that can survive and care for children, we're going to be a real standout city in this entire metropolitan area." The proposals treat child care as infrastructure — if families can't afford it, they can't stay.

**Voting Age and Civic Participation**

The council voted 5-3 to drop the voting age for city elections to 16. Lucas Rigg, 16, said it gave people who didn't choose to live in College Park a say in how it's run. Lovisa Jordan called it cool: "I think it's an option that we can get our voices and opinions heard." College Park became the ninth Maryland city to allow teen voting. Six 16- and 17-year-olds voted in the November election that followed. Overall turnout: 9.9% — 1,598 people.

**School Quality and Educational Partnerships**

When the Prince George's County Board of Education threatened Paint Branch Elementary's Chinese immersion program, the council sent a letter. PTA Secretary Alexandra Tyukavina proposed an alternative: add an opt-out option and a full-day English-speaking teacher rather than eliminating the program. Meanwhile, a citizen committee kept pushing on school bus stop safety — Donald Hays, the committee chair, called the years of inaction "an outrage." The council has influence here but no direct control; county decisions set the terms.

**Economic Development Along Route 1**

Route 1 and Baltimore Avenue are College Park's commercial spine. When Wonder opened a food hall with 15-plus restaurant brands on Baltimore Avenue, Mayor Kabir called it a milestone. Economic Development Director Michael Williams pointed to its location: "the epicenter of downtown College Park." The city also eyes annexation — Jacob Hernandez said they're in early talks about absorbing the soon-to-be-vacant Beltsville Agricultural Research Center, noting that "should a property fall within city limits, the residents of College Park have much more of a say in what gets built there." The city also passed a vacant property registry requiring owners to register empty buildings for $25 with fines for non-compliance.

**Sustainability and Micromobility**

Veo electric scooters and bikes saw 62% ridership growth between fall 2024 and fall 2025. Kabir was enthusiastic: "Every time someone rides a Veo scooter, that person is not using the car." But the scooters were blocking sidewalks and doors, so Veo tightened rules — $4–$5 charges for parking outside designated areas, up to $60 fines for repeat offenders. On Edmonston Road, a city-commissioned noise study found traffic "clearly audible in front yards" after trees were removed between the road and Kenilworth Avenue. Ray Ranker committed to replanting trees and shrubs. Residents wanted traffic-calming devices; John Rigg, then a councilmember and volunteer paramedic, opposed speed humps because they cause pain to patients with hip and pelvis fractures.

## Key Organizations

**College Park City Council** 

The city council makes the core decisions shaping the city's future. The eight-member body has grown younger, with Daniel Oates, Kelly Jordan, and Holly Simmons joining alongside veterans like Kabir, Hernandez, and Hew. They've passed tenant protection ordinances, pushed developers on affordability, lowered the voting age, endorsed the Old Calvert Road bridge, and taken up restorative justice for Lakeland. They're navigating annexation talks, vacant property registries, noise ordinance enforcement, and how to support residents hit by federal job cuts.

**University of Maryland** 

The university dominates College Park's physical and economic landscape. Thousands of students reshape neighborhoods through high rents, tenant turnover, and tensions with long-term residents — a phenomenon the council calls "studentification." Three students presented research showing successful communities use "deep university and city cooperation," but councilmembers like Hew argue UMD should do more. The university partners on projects like the Lakeland murals but leaves many neighborhood problems for the city to solve. Its influence extends to school decisions (Paint Branch's Chinese immersion program draws UMD families), development pressure, and policing — UMD's own police department runs a citizen academy. Track UMD's housing policies, enrollment decisions, and community outreach separately from what councilmembers say about them — the institution is often the story behind the story.

**Prince George's County** 

The county sets the broader policy context — from school board decisions on Paint Branch's immersion program to State Highway Administration noise studies on Kenilworth Avenue. County Councilmember Tom Dernoga's opposition to Moore's density bill reflects county-level resistance to state mandates. The county police department patrols College Park; County Executive Aisha Braveboy mobilizes snow crews. The Maryland-National Capital Park and Planning Commission shapes zoning decisions. College Park exists in this county web, sometimes aligned, sometimes pushing back. For crime data, response times, and safety trends, go directly to county police — Alan Hew is the council liaison, but the department itself is the authoritative source.

**Prince George's County Municipal Association** 

This association hit a crisis when a former treasurer allegedly embezzled over $30,000. Hernandez called it "lacking integrity." PGCMA President Edouard Haba promised financial oversight committees and reviews. The association exists so small cities can "stand together and speak with one voice" — but whether College Park rejoins depends on PGCMA proving it can be trusted.

**Neighborhood civic associations** 

Berwyn District, Calvert Hills, and others — show up repeatedly as organized homeowner voices pushing the council on hyperlocal issues. Donald Hays chaired the school bus stop safety subcommittee and called the years of inaction "an outrage." Calvert Hills residents pushed back on Kenilworth Avenue noise. These associations represent long-term homeowners often at odds with student renters or new construction.

**Prince George's County Board of Education**

PG county's Board of Education controls school policy affecting College Park families. The board's proposal to eliminate Paint Branch Elementary's Chinese immersion program drew council opposition and PTA mobilization. Principal Carlos Johnson at Hollywood Elementary secured a Coca-Cola donation of shoes and supplies for all 301 students — a moment that underlined the need that school fundraising still has to fill.

**Developers and housing companies** 

Article Student Living, Branchville Crossing backers, RST Development's Flats at College Park — face consistent council pressure to include affordable units. The city's tax incentive program makes affordability the price of cooperation. These companies navigate that demand while trying to make projects financially viable.

**State Highway Administration and transportation agencies** 

They control infrastructure that affects College Park daily but sits beyond city control. SHA's noise mitigation decisions matter for Edmonston Road. The Old Calvert Road bridge needs state coordination. Route 1 and Baltimore Avenue projects require state approval and funding. The city is often in a lobbying posture rather than the driver's seat on these.

**College Park Restorative Justice Commission** 

The commission, chaired by Maxine Gross, 20 members strong — is the body pushing the city to move from symbolic acknowledgment of Lakeland's displacement to material reparations. Their recommendations include financial compensation, housing priority, and free college tuition for those who lived there for at least 10 years. They are still waiting for the council to act.

**Lakeland Community Heritage Project**

The project has worked for decades to preserve Lakeland's history and advocate for its displaced residents. The 10-mural project, budgeted at $28,382, represents the most visible recent output. It's not the same as reparations, but it keeps the history visible.

## How We Got Here

College Park's arc over recent years runs from political crisis to cautious rebuilding, with housing pressure and restorative justice as the persistent throughlines.

The sharpest break was Patrick Wojahn's resignation and arrest. The former mayor resigned the night before his March 2 arrest on child pornography charges, was convicted on 60 counts of distribution of child pornography, and is serving 30 years of a 150-year sentence. Denise Mitchell served as pro tem mayor through that period. Kabir won the special election and brought stability, but the rupture matters for understanding why trust and governance reform kept surfacing as council themes in the aftermath.

On the council itself, turnover brought generational change. Llatetra Brown Esters and Susan Whitney left their District 2 seats; Holly Simmons and Kelly Jordan replaced them, Jordan by three votes over Chris Gill after a hand recount. Daniel Oates took District 3 unopposed. The newer members — Oates, Jordan, Simmons — pushed harder on questions about utility bills, school quality, parks, and whether the city's stated commitments were actually being honored.

The voting age debate resolved in 2025 when the council voted 5-3 to let 16- and 17-year-olds vote in city elections. Six teenagers voted in the November election that followed — symbolic, but a signal about who the city considers stakeholders.

The Lakeland restorative justice question moved from background concern to active demand. The 20-member Restorative Justice Commission delivered specific recommendations: financial compensation, housing priority, free college tuition for people who lived there at least 10 years. The murals arrived as a visible first step. The deeper reckoning is still coming.

Housing affordability threads through nearly everything. The tenant protection ordinance passed unanimously. The Branchville Crossing senior housing project secured council support. The Guilford Drive development became a test case for whether the city could extract affordability commitments from a major developer. Governor Moore's statewide density bill put College Park in the middle of a bigger housing fight, with Peña-Melnyk pushing from Annapolis.

Public safety matured without producing a city police force. Alan Hew's citizen academy degrees positioned him as the council's translator between residents and the county and university departments that do the actual patrolling. Oates and Ranker ran on pragmatic alternatives — cameras, blue lights — rather than creating a department. The mechanics of how law enforcement actually works in College Park remain underreported.

The economic development picture brightened with Wonder's arrival on Baltimore Avenue, the BARC annexation talks, and the vacant property registry aimed at reducing blight. Trump administration cuts landed hard — the city doubled its financial assistance budget to $100,000 for residents displaced from BARC and NOAA. Veo's scooter ridership jumped 62% with stricter parking enforcement in response.

The persistent throughlines: housing pressure, student-resident friction, slow infrastructure delivery, and justice deferred for Lakeland. What's rising is impatience — newer council members asking harder questions, residents demanding action rather than studies. What's fading is tolerance for delay.

## Reporting gaps

The Paint Branch Elementary Chinese immersion story got documented when it became a crisis — but no one followed whether the program survived, whether families organized around it stayed or left, or what other programs across the county hang by similar threads. The education beat in College Park appears as isolated moments (shoes from Coca-Cola, council letters supporting schools) rather than a sustained look at how families experience the county's educational bureaucracy from inside city limits.

The University of Maryland shows up everywhere but rarely gets interrogated as an institution. Students present about "studentification" and ask for lease protections. Hew says UMD should teach students to be better neighbors. But does it? What does UMD actually do — or refuse to do — when College Park asks for help with noise, trash, or overcrowded rental houses? When Article Student Living proposes 192 units on Guilford Drive, the university's own development plans may be making that fight harder or easier, and no one's examined the connection. The power imbalance — 40,000 students vs. 34,000 residents — deserves more than passing mentions.

Lakeland's displaced residents remain mostly unnamed beyond Maxine Gross. Who are they? How many still live nearby? What do they think of the commission's specific recommendations — money versus housing priority versus tuition — and what do they think of the pace? Does the younger generation of Lakelanders see this as meaningful accountability or performance?

The financial architecture beneath affordable housing stays unexplained. Branchville Crossing uses 4% federal low-income housing tax credits; Article is being pushed toward city incentive programs. But who actually qualifies for units at 60% of area median income in Prince George's County in 2024? Does that number match what service workers or adjunct professors earn? When developers get pressed to include affordability, do the economics genuinely work without subsidies? The child care task force proposes grants and tax credits without anyone explaining how the city pays for them. Someone should map what "affordable" means across all these programs and whether the math adds up to enough housing for the people being priced out.

The Prince George's County Police Department's relationship with College Park gets named but not examined. When the council talks about cameras and blue light phones instead of a city force, what does enforcement actually look like day-to-day? How quickly do county officers respond to College Park calls? Do residents in North College Park get the same service as those in Berwyn? The council talks about safety constantly; the mechanics of who actually provides it stay underreported.

Route 1 and Baltimore Avenue appear as location markers but never get treated as subjects. What do businesses along the corridor actually earn? How many are locally owned? When Wonder's arrival is called a milestone, does that mean previous retail recruitment failed — and why? The corridor shapes the tax base and the city's identity, but coverage stays relentlessly event-based rather than treating the strip as a story in itself.
