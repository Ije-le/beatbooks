## Executive Snapshot

# Executive Snapshot

College Park is a city perpetually negotiating its own identity — caught between the gravitational pull of the University of Maryland, the displacement ghosts of Lakeland, and the relentless economics of the Route 1 corridor. The city council that governs this 34,000-person community operates at the intersection of high-stakes housing politics, restorative justice debates that reach back decades, and the grinding daily work of snow removal, noise complaints, and bus stop safety. Fazlul Kabir, the unopposed mayor who has racked up more than a decade in city leadership, presides over a council that has skewed younger in recent elections — bringing in voices like Jacob Hernandez, who graduated from citizen police academies to speak confidently about public safety, and newcomers like Kelly Jordan, whose three-vote margin of victory after a hand recount suggests just how razor-thin democratic mandates can be. The data spans a period when the council passed an early lease ordinance to protect student tenants pressured to re-sign within weeks of moving in, lowered the voting age to 16, and paused its membership in a countywide municipal association after an embezzlement scandal. It also captures the council wrestling with whether to annex the soon-to-be-vacant Beltsville Agricultural Research Center, whether to double councilmember pay from $10,000 to $20,000, and whether to require vacant property owners to register their empty buildings for $25.

The most persistent tensions here are about who belongs and who pays. The Restorative Justice Commission, chaired by Maxine Gross, has urged the council to provide reparations — financial compensation, housing priority, free college tuition — to displaced Lakeland residents whose vibrant neighborhood was erased by urban renewal between 1969 and 1981. At the same time, Chicago-based developer Article Student Living is planning a 192-unit complex on Guilford Drive, and the council, led by Denise Mitchell's insistence that "we're not going to negate that vision," is pushing developers to include affordable units in exchange for tax incentives. Students, many living in downtown high-rises, show up to council meetings to describe landlords pressuring them to renew leases before they've unpacked their boxes. Residents of Edmonston Road complain that traffic noise is "clearly audible in the front yards" after the city removed trees between their street and Kenilworth Avenue. And through it all, the University of Maryland looms — a force that councilmember Alan Hew insists should "take more responsibility for teaching its students how to be good neighbors," even as the city relies on student rent, student labor, and student votes (now including 16- and 17-year-olds) to sustain its economy and civic life.

The people who hold power here are a mix of long-serving insiders and recent arrivals testing the system's responsiveness. John Rigg, a volunteer paramedic for 16 years, once voted against speed humps on Autoville Drive because he knows what a fractured pelvis feels like over a bump. Ray Ranker has been asking for a bridge connecting Old Calvert Road Park to the Northeast Branch Trail for at least a decade, and he's not interested in waiting another month. Joseline Peña-Melnyk, a former councilmember, now serves as Speaker of the Maryland House of Delegates and represents College Park in Annapolis, where she champions Governor Wes Moore's push to allow eight houses per acre — a proposal that State Sen. Jim Rosapepe and County Councilmember Tom Dernoga oppose over fears of congestion and pollution. The city's relationship with Prince George's County is constant but uneven: the council sends letters to the county school board to save the Paint Branch Chinese immersion program, relies on county police for public safety, and navigates the sprawling authority of the Maryland-National Capital Park and Planning Commission. Meanwhile, organizations like the Berwyn District Civic Association and Calvert Hills Citizens Association shape neighborhood-level debates, and Michael Williams, the city's economic development director, helps court chains like Wonder, which just opened its first Maryland food hall on Baltimore Avenue — a project John Rigg said the city "hit out of the park."

## Top Recurring Issues

# Top Recurring Issues

**Affordable Housing**

The question of where people can afford to live runs through nearly every major development conversation in College Park. As more projects rise along Route 1 and developers pitch new complexes, the city council keeps pushing back with the same challenge: What will you build for people who can't pay market rates?

The tension becomes visible at moments like the Article Student Living meeting, when officials pressed the Chicago-based developer to include affordable units in their 192-unit Guilford Drive complex. Mayor Fazlul Kabir laid out the city's position plainly: "The catch is that you must have affordable housing in it." Councilmember Denise Mitchell emphasized the stakes: "We have a vision moving forward, and we're not going to negate that vision."

That vision includes expanding the city's existing affordable housing stock — places like the Flats at College Park, a 317-unit complex with income-restricted units financed through federal Low-Income Housing Tax Credits. An affordable senior housing project on Branchville Road brought 87 units for residents 62 and older, with rent based on 60% of area median income. Councilmember Kelly Jordan captured the need: "There is a real need for senior housing in College Park."

The issue also surfaces in statewide debates. When Governor Wes Moore proposed changing Maryland zoning law to allow developers to build at least eight houses per acre, House Speaker Joseline Peña-Melnyk, who represents College Park, pushed hard for it: "Maryland has a housing shortage of roughly 100,000 homes. We need more housing choices. Smaller homes. Townhouses. Accessory dwelling units." But State Sen. Jim Rosapepe and County Councilmember Tom Dernoga pushed back, citing concerns about congestion and infrastructure strain.

A child care task force even framed the problem as an economic development issue — if College Park can make housing and child care affordable together, "we're going to be a real standout city in this entire metropolitan area," said task force member Gavin Neubauer.

The pressure won't ease. As developers keep coming, the city keeps asking the same question: Who gets to live here?

**Tenant Protections and Student Housing**

When Sami Saeed, a University of Maryland public policy major, got approached by his landlord to renew his lease just four weeks after moving in — with a substantial rent increase attached — he scrambled. "The stress that that caused me … trying to find anyone to live with for next year. I had to call 50 people."

His story became the centerpiece of a council debate that led to the city unanimously passing an ordinance preventing landlords from asking tenants to renew a lease sooner than 180 days from the end of the current lease. Nearly 30 people, mostly student tenants from downtown high-rises, packed council chambers to share similar experiences. Nisha Kumar captured the bind: "We've just moved in for the semester and we already have to figure out next year... Who's going to take my spot? ... Will I have to pay over $1,000 a month for a place I'm not even living in?" Scott Tebbe called the pressure "predatory": "This was a decision I wasn't ready to make, and I felt trapped."

The ordinance fines landlords $500 for the first offense and $2,500 for repeat violations. Some landlords argued it would limit their control over tenant selection, but the council sided with students facing what they saw as exploitation in a tight housing market.

The student housing question extends beyond leases. Three University of Maryland students — Frances Chen, Nicholas Crane, and Ashna Pandya — presented to the council about neighborhood tensions caused by "studentification" of established areas and high rents. Chen's research showed that "the most successful communities have used deep university and city cooperation to create a solution that works for both students and [homeowners] and balances affordable housing for the student population [with] enforcing city codes." Councilmember Alan Hew pushed back on the university: it should take more responsibility for teaching students how to be good neighbors.

When Article Student Living proposed 192 units on Guilford Drive to replace Hope Lutheran Church, the developer faced the same affordability pressure. The city's relationship with student landlords remains transactional — each project an opportunity to extract concessions for tenants who have little leverage on their own.

**Restorative Justice for Lakeland**

The displacement of Lakeland's Black residents through urban renewal between 1969 and 1981 refuses to stay in the past. Maxine Gross, chair of the 20-member Restorative Justice Commission, keeps bringing it forward: "When you talk to people who experienced Lakeland before urban renewal, they speak of a vibrant community."

That community was erased. Now the commission is pushing for reparations — financial compensation, housing priority, and free college tuition for those who lived in Lakeland for at least 10 years or their direct descendants. The recommendations aren't symbolic. They're specific, costed, and waiting for the city council to decide whether to act.

Meanwhile, Lakeland's history is becoming visible in a different way. The neighborhood is adding 10 murals to highlight what was lost. Gross framed the project as essential memory work: "It's just a way for folks to be able to see the community that we have been talking about for a number of years." Mayor Fazlul Kabir called it "a wonderful and very easy and entertaining way to know about what happened and what used to be the historic Lakeland." The project, a joint effort among city officials, community members, and University of Maryland partners, carries a $28,382 price tag.

The murals tell stories. The reparations would cost real money. Candidates for District 2 council seats all agreed that restorative justice for Lakeland matters — Holly Simmons said "reparations are very important," and Kelly Jordan suggested new housing developments should include affordable units as part of that justice.

The question isn't whether Lakeland should be remembered. It's whether the city will pay what the commission says is owed.

**Public Safety Without a City Police Force**

College Park navigates public safety without its own police department, relying instead on Prince George's County Police, University of Maryland Police, and the county sheriff's office. That arrangement creates ongoing questions about response times, accountability, and whether the city has enough say in how it's policed.

Councilmember Alan Hew made himself an expert. He graduated from three different citizen police academies — Prince George's County Police, University of Maryland Police, and the county sheriff's office. "There's a lot of speculation of how they operate and I wanted to be able to speak confidently in support of the departments that operate within our city," Hew explained. His training lets him contribute to council safety discussions with firsthand knowledge and help constituents understand police operations.

When District 3 candidates Dan Oates and Ray Ranker debated public safety, they both rejected creating a city police force. Oates said "jumping to the answer of 'we need a police force' is premature." Ranker suggested measures like additional security cameras or blue light phones might work better. The candidates framed the debate around effectiveness and cost, not principle.

Parking enforcement offers a glimpse of how the city handles some public safety functions directly. Officer Jim Miller proposed expanding permit parking in residential neighborhoods, telling the council "It's a matter of how that restricted parking is utilized." Former Councilmember Susan Whitney suggested a different approach — incentives like tax breaks to encourage residents to own fewer cars: "We want people who need their cars to be able to park them, and we want people who can get by with one car to do that and to be encouraged to do that."

The city also tackled specific safety infrastructure. A citizen-led committee, frustrated by inaction, resubmitted a report on school bus stop safety recommending upgrades at seven stops that scored low on evaluations. Committee chairman Donald Hays didn't hide his irritation: "I personally feel that is an outrage and there has been very little movement and very little progress." The recommendations included adding bike lanes and sidewalks to protect elementary school children waiting for buses.

College Park's safety strategy remains patchwork — partnerships with other departments, citizen committees pushing for infrastructure improvements, and elected officials trying to build trust without direct control over the officers who patrol their streets.

**Economic Development Along Route 1**

Route 1 and Baltimore Avenue form College Park's economic spine, and the city treats every new business opening or development proposal as a chance to reshape what downtown looks like. When the food hall chain Wonder opened its first Maryland location on Baltimore Avenue, offering food from over 15 restaurants and brands, Councilmember John Rigg celebrated the city's work "to bring an exciting concept here," adding "I think they hit it out of the park." Mayor Kabir called it "a milestone." Economic development director Michael Williams emphasized the location as "the epicenter of downtown College Park."

Development proposals along the corridor trigger immediate questions about what the city gets in return. The Guilford Drive student housing project faced pressure to include affordable units. The Branchville Road senior housing project moved forward partly because it used federal low-income housing tax credits and served a clear need. When developers come to College Park, they don't just pitch projects — they negotiate what the city's vision demands they include.

That vision extends beyond buildings. The city created a Live+Work College Park program offering zero-interest loans to full-time employees who buy homes in the city, trying to tie economic development to residential stability. According to Danny Copeland, Vice President for RST Development, projects like the Flats at College Park get financed partly through federal Low-Income Housing Tax Credits, showing how the city layers different funding mechanisms to achieve affordability goals.

The city also eyes future opportunities through annexation. Officials are considering bringing in multiple properties, including the soon-to-be-vacant Beltsville Agricultural Research Center. Councilmember Jacob Hernandez explained the strategy: "Should a property fall within the city limits, the residents of College Park have much more of a say in what gets built there." Annexation becomes a tool for control — the city expands its borders to ensure it can shape development rather than watch it happen in unincorporated areas beyond council reach.

Route 1 development keeps the council busy because every project represents competing interests: developers seeking profit, the city demanding affordability and amenities, existing residents worried about density and parking, and students needing housing they can actually afford.

**City Code Enforcement and Quality of Life**

The city keeps tightening its code enforcement tools, raising fines and creating new registration requirements to address problems that elected officials and residents say degrade neighborhood quality. Director of Public Services Jatinder Khokhar and City Attorney Stephanie Anderson presented a proposal to increase fines for city code violations — including construction, fire safety, housing, noise, and unruly social gatherings — to align with Maryland law allowing municipalities to fine up to $5,000. Councilmember John Rigg drew a distinction: "Noise should be bad, but unruly social gatherings almost always include noise and it includes a lot more."

Vacant properties became a specific target. The council voted to require owners of vacant homes and commercial property to register their empty buildings with the city, paying $25 and facing fines for non-compliance. Mayor Kabir explained the rationale: "Vacant properties can negatively impact our community... When left unmonitored, they can become safety hazards, attract vandalism and contribute to blight." Councilmember Hernandez added that the ordinance "supports improving public safety... It protects property values."

Noise complaints reveal how quality-of-life enforcement intersects with infrastructure decisions. A city-commissioned noise analysis by RK&K found that traffic noise is "clearly audible in the front yards" on Edmonston Road and warrants a more detailed study and potential mitigation by the State Highway Administration. Residents like Maria Cordone complained that noise increased after the city removed trees between Edmonston Road and Kenilworth Avenue. Councilmember Ray Ranker said the city would plant trees and shrubs between the two streets to help with sound and beautification, while some residents suggested the state install traffic-calming devices.

Even micromobility created enforcement challenges. Veo, the electric scooter and bike provider, implemented stricter parking rules after use increased 62% between fall 2024 and fall 2025. Alex Posorske, Veo's East Coast Policy and Partnerships Manager, explained that the company now charges riders an extra $4 to $5 for parking outside designated areas and fines customers up to $60 for repeated violations. Mayor Kabir supported Veo despite the parking problems: "Every time someone rides a Veo scooter, that person is not using the car, right? So we're taking out the cars from the street."

The council's enforcement strategy assumes that higher fines and registration requirements will change behavior — that landlords will maintain properties, partygoers will keep noise down, and scooter riders will park correctly if the financial consequences rise high enough. Whether enforcement capacity matches the city's ambitions remains an open question.

**Community Engagement and Representation**

College Park lowered its voting age for city elections from 18 to 16 in a 5-3 council vote, making it the ninth Maryland city to allow teenagers to vote for mayor and councilmembers. Lucas Rigg, 16, captured what the change means: "I think it lets the people who didn't choose to live here have a say." Lovisa Jordan, also 16, saw it as both empowerment and education: "I think it's cool... I think that it's an option that we can get our voices and opinions heard" and that preparing to vote could make teenagers more aware of what's happening in the city. In the first election after the change, six teenagers aged 16 and 17 cast ballots out of 1,598 total voters, representing 9.9% of registered voters.

The composition of the council itself shifted younger with the election of Daniel Oates, Kelly Jordan, and Holly Simmons, joining existing members like Jacob Hernandez and Ray Ranker. Oates downplayed age as the key factor: "To me what's interesting is less [about] the relative ages than the new people coming on the council and bringing different experiences and different perspectives from that vein." Jordan emphasized representation as central to her campaign: "I want to make sure that there are lots of different interests that all get heard from and incorporated into the decision-making."

That representation extends to making council positions financially viable for a broader range of residents. The council considered doubling part-time salaries from $10,000 to $20,000 a year. Hernandez made the case: "Everyone on this dais works incredibly hard for the residents that we care for," adding that the proposed increase "might not be extraordinarily significant, but it does help cover the costs." Ranker agreed it might attract more candidates: "If the pay was $1 million, more people would probably run for office than if the pay were $0 per year."

Community engagement shows up in formal structures like the Child Care Task Force, which proposed nearly 20 actions including grants to home-based providers and property tax credits to established centers. It appears in citizen committees like the school bus stop safety group, whose chairman Donald Hays publicly expressed frustration at city inaction. It surfaces when Paint Branch Elementary PTA Secretary Alexandra Tyukavina proposed an alternative to the county school board's plan to eliminate the Chinese immersion program, finding a way to address budget issues without destroying the program: "We, the Paint Branch school community, have found that the board's proposal to eliminate the program is not the only way to address budget issues, nor is it ideal for the students and families within the school."

The university students who presented on neighborhood tensions, the 30 tenants who demanded early lease protections, the Lakeland residents pushing for reparations — all represent different forms of community engagement. The council's challenge is deciding which voices to prioritize when they conflict.

**Local Business Support and Community Anchors**

Small businesses and long-standing community institutions get public celebration even as the city pursues larger economic development. When Queens Chapel Barbershop celebrated its 85th anniversary, city councilmembers Joshua Solomon and Kelson Nisbett attended along with Hyattsville Police Chief Jarod Towers. Customer Merrill Hartson, a retired AP News editor, gave remarks comparing the shop to the TV show Cheers: "For 85 years, the Queens Chapel Barbershop has persevered, never short of sheers, never at a loss for cheers."

Hollywood Elementary School received 301 pairs of new sneakers, socks, and school supplies from the local Coca-Cola bottler through the Back-to-School with Purpose initiative. Principal Carlos Johnson described the impact: "When you can provide for a parent and the things that they need at home, school and home become one." Councilmember Jacob Hernandez praised Johnson's community-building work: "He's gone above and beyond to make sure he's providing meaningful outreach."

The city threw its support behind Paint Branch Elementary's Chinese immersion program when the county school board proposed eliminating it for budget reasons. The council voted to send a letter supporting continued funding. The program had become "an important part of the broader educational ecosystem serving College Park residents," and the council argued that "changes that significantly reduce immersion opportunities could undermine years of investment by educators, students and the community."

These moments of support cost the city little directly — a letter here, attendance at an anniversary there. But they signal what the city values beyond tax revenue and development fees. When the Article Student Living developer proposed replacing Hope Lutheran Church with student housing, the plan included a new church space. Pastor Julie Bringman noted the new space would be larger, fully accessible, and might have room for offices or affordable housing units — preserving a community institution while enabling development.

The challenge for College Park is maintaining these anchors as development pressure increases property values and long-time businesses face higher costs. Celebration doesn't stop displacement. But it does mark what the community says it wants to keep.

## Power Map

**Fazlul Kabir**

The mayor of College Park holds a position central to nearly every civic initiative in the city, from affordable housing development to snow removal operations. Kabir has led the city through a period of significant change, pushing for stronger finances, new community spaces, and job creation while navigating the unpredictable impacts of federal policy shifts during the Trump administration's second term. When President Trump's executive orders began affecting local federal employees, Kabir responded by expanding the city's financial assistance program to $100,000, helping residents who lost jobs at places like the Beltsville Agricultural Research Center. He's championed sustainability initiatives, defending Veo's micromobility system by noting that every scooter ride removes a car from the street, and he's pushed developers toward the city's vision of affordable housing, telling Article Student Living that the city's tax incentive program requires affordable units. Kabir supported the Chinese immersion program at Paint Branch Elementary, viewing educational investment as crucial to the community's future. He also celebrated the Lakeland murals project as a way to preserve heritage and make history accessible. Running unopposed for re-election, Kabir has positioned himself as someone who strengthens infrastructure—he highlighted completing the first phase of a major Baltimore Avenue project that improved safety and traffic flow—while keeping the city responsive to resident needs, whether that means posting interactive snow plow maps or urging people to report icy sidewalks.

**Jacob Hernandez**

A city councilmember who emerged as one of the top vote-getters in recent elections with 536 votes, Hernandez has built a reputation for accountability and direct engagement with constituent concerns. He pressed the council to pause membership in the Prince George's County Municipal Association after a former treasurer allegedly embezzled over $30,000, arguing that taxpayer money shouldn't go to an organization "clearly lacking integrity." His focus on financial stewardship extends to advocating for a doubled salary for councilmembers—from $10,000 to $20,000 annually—acknowledging that while the increase "might not be extraordinarily significant," it helps cover costs and might attract more community members to run for office. Hernandez has shown particular interest in education and community support, praising Hollywood Elementary Principal Carlos Johnson for going "above and beyond" to build local relationships and secure resources like the Coca-Cola sneaker donation for all 301 students. He's also exploring the strategic possibilities of annexation, particularly regarding the soon-to-be-vacant Beltsville Agricultural Research Center, explaining that bringing properties within city limits gives College Park residents "much more of a say in what gets built there." His approach combines fiscal watchfulness with a vision for controlled growth that prioritizes community input.

The council has tackled an extraordinary range of issues: lowering the voting age to 16, creating an early lease ordinance to protect student tenants, requiring vacant property registration, considering doubled council salaries, pausing membership in a regional association after embezzlement allegations, and endorsing everything from Chinese immersion programs to Old Calvert Road bridges. The council's composition shifted younger with recent elections, bringing in people like Jordan, Oates, and Simmons, who join existing members in wrestling with how to accommodate student housing demand while preserving neighborhood character, how to attract development while mandating affordability, and how to improve safety without creating a city police force. Decisions often expose tensions: some councilmembers want aggressive penalties for code violations while others distinguish between noise complaints and unruly gatherings; some supported speed humps while Rigg argued they hurt patients in ambulances. The council operates as both a governing body and a forum where university students, displaced Lakeland residents, elementary school parents, senior citizens, and neighborhood associations all compete for attention and resources.

## Geographic Hotspots

The College Park beat revolves around a handful of arterial streets and historically contested neighborhoods where development pressures, public safety anxieties, and community identity collide. Some locations appear constantly in coverage because they sit at the intersection of multiple policy fights — affordable housing, student behavior, traffic safety, historical preservation. **Route 1 / Baltimore Avenue**

This corridor functions as College Park's commercial spine and political testing ground. When Wonder food hall opened on Baltimore Avenue, Councilmember John Rigg called it hitting the project "out of the park" — a milestone for the city's economic development ambitions. Mayor Fazlul Kabir described Baltimore Avenue as "the epicenter of downtown College Park," a location developers eye for its proximity to the University of Maryland and its potential to anchor a more vibrant, less car-dependent city center. **Lakeland**

Lakeland is not simply a neighborhood — it's the city's unresolved moral crisis. Between 1969 and 1981, urban renewal displaced a historically Black community, scattering residents and erasing a "vibrant community," as Maxine Gross, chair of the Restorative Justice Commission, described it. Gross and the 20-member commission are now pushing the council for reparations: financial compensation, housing priority, free college tuition for those who lived there at least 10 years or their direct descendants. The council added 10 murals to the area, part of the Lakeland Community Heritage Project, with Gross noting it's "a way for folks to be able to see the community that we have been talking about for a number of years." Mayor Kabir called the murals "a wonderful and very easy and entertaining way to know about what happened and what used to be the historic Lakeland." The tension here is stark — memorializing history through murals while the displaced wait for substantive restitution. Reporters should monitor how the council responds to the commission's recommendations. Will the city offer real repair, or will it settle for symbolic acknowledgment? Lakeland exposes whether College Park's progressive rhetoric on equity translates into material action.

**Edmonston Road**

Edmonston Road is where infrastructure decisions become lived misery. After the city removed trees between Edmonston Road and Kenilworth Avenue, residents like Maria Cordone complained that traffic noise became overwhelming. A city-commissioned analysis by RK&K found noise "clearly audible in the front yards" and recommended further study and potential mitigation by the State Highway Administration. Councilmember Ray Ranker said the city plans to plant trees and shrubs to help with sound and beautification, and some residents suggested the state install traffic-calming devices to stop speeders. The situation illustrates how technical decisions — tree removal, highway proximity — cascade into quality-of-life crises. Reporters should follow whether the state acts on mitigation and whether the city's replanting effort delivers meaningful relief. Edmonston Road is a case study in how infrastructure policy affects residents asymmetrically, often leaving those near major roadways to absorb the costs of regional traffic patterns.

**Branchville Road**

Branchville Road became a focal point when the council agreed to amend a parking ordinance to allow a four-story, 87-unit apartment building for residents 62 and older. The project, Branchville Crossing, provides affordable housing for seniors with rent based on 60 percent of area median income, financed through a 4 percent federal low-income housing tax credit. Councilmember Kelly Jordan said, "There is a real need for senior housing in College Park," and noted the developer "listened to the residents… to see what would fit here, what's needed here." The project represents one of the city's few concrete affordable housing wins, but it also reveals the gap between need and supply. Jordan's comment about listening to residents suggests this kind of project required negotiation and compromise. Reporters should track whether Branchville Crossing sets a precedent for future affordable senior housing or remains an isolated success. The location matters because it shows the council can act on affordability when political will and developer interest align.

**Guilford Drive**

Guilford Drive is where the city's affordable housing vision meets developer resistance. ## Timeline and Trend Shifts

# Timeline and Trend Shifts

The College Park beat has undergone a fundamental transformation in how the city sees itself — from managing the friction of being a college town to reimagining what kind of community it wants to become. That shift plays out in policy debates, electoral politics, and the questions residents bring to council chambers.

The voting age debate marked a turning point. When the council voted 5-3 to let 16- and 17-year-olds cast ballots in city elections, it wasn't just procedural tinkering. Lucas Rigg put it plainly: "I think it lets the people who didn't choose to live here have a say." That line captures the city's evolving relationship with its youngest residents — acknowledging that teenagers living in College Park exist in a political landscape shaped by decisions they had no hand in making. Six teens voted in the first election under the new rules, a small number that nonetheless signals the city's willingness to experiment with expanding democratic participation. Whether this becomes a model or a footnote depends on turnout and whether those young voters feel their participation mattered.

Affordable housing has moved from rhetorical priority to concrete policy fight. The early lease ordinance — preventing landlords from forcing renewal decisions within 180 days of lease end — came after Sami Saeed told the council he'd been approached four weeks after moving in with a steep rent increase. "The stress that that caused me… trying to find anyone to live with for next year. I had to call 50 people," he said. The council passed it unanimously, but the larger struggle over affordability has only intensified. Nearly 30 people, mostly students, packed a meeting to demand protection from what they called predatory lease agreements. Nisha Kumar asked the room: "We've just moved in for the semester and we already have to figure out next year... Who's going to take my spot?... Will I have to pay over $1,000 a month for a place I'm not even living in?"

That frustration now collides with development proposals at every council meeting. When Article Student Living pitched a 192-unit complex on Guilford Drive, Mayor Fazlul Kabir pushed them toward the city's tax incentive program: "The catch is that you must have affordable housing in it." Denise Mitchell backed him up: "We have a vision moving forward, and we're not going to negate that vision." The Branchville Crossing senior housing project — 87 units for residents 62 and older, rent based on 60 percent of area median income — shows the city putting money behind that vision, but tension remains over whether new development serves existing residents or accelerates displacement.

Governor Wes Moore's push to allow eight houses per acre statewide landed awkwardly in College Park. House Speaker Joseline Peña-Melnyk, a College Park resident and former councilmember, championed it: "Maryland has a housing shortage of roughly 100,000 homes. We need more housing choices. Smaller homes. Townhouses. Accessory dwelling units." But State Sen. Jim Rosapepe and County Councilmember Tom Dernoga resisted, Dernoga citing concerns about congestion, pollution, and public safety. That split — between state-level housing policy and local control — will shape every zoning fight ahead.

Restorative justice for Lakeland has emerged as the city's most morally urgent unfinished business. The 20-member Restorative Justice Commission recommended reparations for residents displaced by urban renewal between 1969 and 1981. Maxine Gross, the commission chair, framed it simply: "When you talk to people who experienced Lakeland before urban renewal, they speak of a vibrant community." The recommendations — financial compensation, housing priority, free college tuition for those who lived there at least 10 years or their direct descendants — force the city to reckon with decisions made decades ago that hollowed out a Black neighborhood. The Lakeland Community Heritage Project is adding 10 murals to preserve what remains of that history. As Gross said, "It's just a way for folks to be able to see the community that we have been talking about for a number of years." The council has yet to act on the commission's proposals, and that delay will define how seriously the city takes its own rhetoric about equity.

The student-resident divide has shifted from abstract complaint to specific policy experiments. Frances Chen, Nicholas Crane, and Ashna Pandya told the council that "the most successful communities have used deep university and city cooperation to create a solution that works for both students and [homeowners] and balances affordable housing for the student population [with] enforcing city codes." But Alan Hew pushed back, arguing the university should teach students how to be good neighbors. That tension — whether the city addresses studentification through regulation, cooperation, or displacement — remains unresolved. The early lease ordinance helps students; permit parking expansion debates pit neighborhood stability against student mobility. Jim Miller's proposal to study restricted parking sparked immediate conflict over whether to charge for permits. Susan Whitney suggested tax breaks for residents who own fewer cars: "We want people who need their cars to be able to park them, and we want people who can get by with one car to do that and to be encouraged to do that."

Public safety debates have grown more nuanced. The city has resisted creating its own police force despite recurring safety concerns. Dan Oates said flatly, "I think jumping to the answer of 'we need a police force' is premature." Ray Ranker suggested security cameras and blue light phones might work better. Alan Hew has become the council's unofficial police liaison, having graduated from three citizen police academies. "There's a lot of speculation of how they operate and I wanted to be able to speak confidently in support of the departments that operate within our city," he explained. That knowledge base matters when residents demand action without necessarily wanting a municipal force. Veo's crackdown on scooter parking — charging $4 to $5 for violations, fines up to $60 for repeat offenders — shows how the city manages safety and quality-of-life issues through private regulation rather than enforcement infrastructure.

The council's generational shift accelerated after the November election brought in Daniel Oates, Kelly Jordan, and Holly Simmons. Jordan won her seat by three votes after a recount. As Oates noted, "To me what's interesting is less [about] the relative ages than the new people coming on the council and bringing different experiences and different perspectives from that vein." That matters because newer councilmembers are asking questions about utility bills, school quality, parks, and safety that longtime members may have stopped asking. Jordan framed it as fresh eyes: "We'll all have lots of questions and… when you're used to something, comfortable with something, new questions can bring new ideas for how you're used to looking at it."

Education policy has become a surprise battleground. The council voted to support Paint Branch Elementary's Chinese immersion program after the county school board proposed cuts. Alexandra Tyukavina, the PTA secretary, offered a third alternative — an opt-out option and a full-day English teacher for non-immersion students. The council's intervention signals its willingness to challenge county decisions that affect College Park families, a shift from deference to direct advocacy. The citizen-led committee on school bus stop safety shows the same dynamic. Donald Hays, the subcommittee chair, expressed frustration that the city ignored their recommendations: "I personally feel that is an outrage and there has been very little movement and very little progress."

Economic development has moved from attracting any business to curating what fits the city's identity. The Wonder food hall opening on Baltimore Avenue drew praise from John Rigg — "I think they hit it out of the park" — and Michael Williams noted the location is "the epicenter of downtown College Park." But the city is also tightening code enforcement. The proposal to increase fines for violations, including construction, fire safety, housing, noise, and unruly social gatherings, reflects a shift toward holding property owners and businesses accountable. As Rigg noted, "Noise should be bad, but unruly social gatherings almost always include noise and it includes a lot more."

Infrastructure and environmental sustainability run through everything now. The council endorsed a $2 million bridge connecting Old Calvert Road Park to the Northeast Branch Trail after a decade of community requests. Ray Ranker said bluntly, "We don't want to spend another month waiting, we want to get [construction] started… tomorrow." Kabir's support for Veo scooters — "Every time someone rides a Veo scooter, that person is not using the car, right? So we're taking out the cars from the street" — ties micromobility to the city's strategic sustainability goals. But the noise analysis of Edmonston Road shows environmental promises colliding with past decisions. Residents like Maria Cordone complained that noise increased after the city removed trees between Edmonston Road and Kenilworth Avenue. The RK&K study found traffic noise "clearly audible in the front yards" and recommended mitigation. Ranker said the city plans to plant trees and shrubs between the two streets, but some residents want the state to install traffic-calming devices. That back-and-forth — fix it with landscaping or force the state to act — will recur as the city balances development with livability.

Trump's second term introduced federal instability the city can't control. Kabir acknowledged the uncertainty: "We do not know how things will actually go in the future." The city increased its financial assistance budget to $100,000 to help residents affected by layoffs at the Beltsville Agricultural Research Center and NOAA. The council's pause on membership in the Prince George's County Municipal Association after a treasurer allegedly embezzled over $30,000 shows the city rethinking its regional partnerships. Jacob Hernandez asked the obvious question: "It's our taxpayer money... Why would we give money to an organization that is clearly lacking integrity?"

Child care costs are gaining traction as a policy priority. The Child Care Task Force proposed grants to home-based providers and property tax credits to established centers. Gavin Neubauer, a task force member, framed it as competitive advantage: "If you're able to even increase the amount of businesses here that can survive and care for children, we're going to be a real standout city in this entire metropolitan area." The city has 18 licensed child care providers — 11 day care centers, five home-based providers, two tax-exempt religious organizations — and the task force recommended nearly 20 actions to increase availability and lower costs. Whether that translates into budget allocations will test the council's priorities.

Annexation debates are simmering. The city is considering annexing the soon-to-be-vacant BARC land and other nearby properties. Hernandez explained the stakes: "Should a property fall within the city limits, the residents of College Park have much more of a say in what gets built there." That fight — protecting land, driving economic development, boosting population — could reshape the city's borders and tax base, but it's still in early stages.

Persistent gaps remain. The vacant property registration ordinance passed, requiring owners to pay $25 to register empty buildings and face fines for non-compliance. Kabir justified it: "Vacant properties can negatively impact our community... When left unmonitored, they can become safety hazards, attract vandalism and contribute to blight." But enforcement will determine whether it's effective or symbolic. The council's consideration of doubling councilmember salaries from $10,000 to $20,000 a year reflects a broader question about who can afford to serve. Hernandez argued, "Everyone on this dais works incredibly hard for the residents that we care for," and suggested the increase "might not be extraordinarily significant, but it does help cover the costs." Ranker agreed, noting that "if the pay was $1 million, more people would probably run for office than if the pay were $0 per year." That debate about compensation ties directly to representation — whether the council reflects the community or only those with financial cushion to serve.

The beat now demands reporters who can track policy experiments as they unfold — voting age expansion, restorative justice, affordability mandates, regional housing fights, federal instability, infrastructure promises delayed or delivered. The city is testing ideas other jurisdictions watch. Some will fail. Some will become models. A reporter covering this beat today is documenting a city trying to reconcile its past, manage its present, and decide what it wants to become — all while negotiating with a university, a county, a state, and a federal government that don't always share its vision.

Hernandez also co-chairs discussions on annexation of properties like the Beltsville Agricultural Research Center, emphasizing resident control over development. He won the most votes in recent elections and appears deeply engaged in both policy minutiae and constituent concerns. He offers a window into council debates, dissenting voices, and the mechanics of governance.

**John Rigg, City Councilmember and Volunteer Paramedic**

Rigg brings unusual expertise to the dais: 16 years as a volunteer paramedic with Calvert Advanced Life Support. This shapes his perspective on traffic calming, leading him to oppose speed humps on Autoville Drive because of the pain they cause patients with fractures. His dual role as councilmember and emergency responder gives him credibility on public safety discussions and a street-level understanding of how policy plays out in moments of crisis. Rigg also celebrated Wonder's arrival in College Park, saying the city "hit it out of the park" with the food hall concept. He represents a blend of pragmatic governance and hands-on community service that can help reporters understand how abstract decisions affect real people.

**Alan Hew, City Councilmember and Citizen Police Academy Graduate**

Hew has graduated from three separate citizen police academies — Prince George's County Police, University of Maryland Police, and the Prince George's County Sheriff's Office. He did this, he says, to "speak confidently in support of the departments that operate within our city" and to counter speculation about how police operate. This makes Hew the council's de facto public safety expert, someone who can explain police procedures, build trust with officers, and help constituents understand law enforcement operations. He also emphasized that the University of Maryland should teach students how to be good neighbors, pointing to tensions in residential areas near campus. Hew's deep knowledge of policing and his willingness to hold institutions accountable make him essential for stories on crime, enforcement, and university-community relations.

**Denise Mitchell, City Councilmember**

Mitchell speaks with authority about the city's vision for development and affordable housing. When Article Student Living proposed a 192-unit complex on Guilford Drive, Mitchell made clear the council's expectations: "We have a vision moving forward, and we're not going to negate that vision." She appears to be a guardian of the city's long-term planning goals, ensuring developers align with College Park's affordable housing ambitions. Mitchell also participated in discussions about senior housing on Branchville Road, signaling her engagement with demographic diversity and equity. She represents the council's policy backbone — the voice that reminds developers and staff what the city has promised itself.

**Maxine Gross, Lakeland Community Heritage Project Member**

Gross chairs the Restorative Justice Commission and speaks powerfully about Lakeland's displacement during urban renewal from 1969 to 1981. She described the community before its destruction: "When you talk to people who experienced Lakeland before urban renewal, they speak of a vibrant community." Gross led the commission's push for reparations, including financial compensation, housing priority, and free college tuition for displaced residents or their descendants. She also championed the mural project bringing 10 artworks to Lakeland to preserve its history. Gross embodies the community's moral memory and its demand for accountability. She is essential for understanding College Park's reckoning with its past and the unfinished work of restorative justice.

**City of College Park Public Services and Planning Offices**

The city's administrative apparatus — particularly Public Services Director Jatinder Khokhar and former Economic Development Director Michael Williams — drives the mechanics of governance. Khokhar has presented proposals to increase fines for code violations, aligning city penalties with state law. Williams positioned Wonder's food hall as a perfect fit for "the epicenter of downtown College Park." These offices generate the ordinances, development pitches, and enforcement policies that shape daily life. Reporters should check in regularly with planning staff, public services leadership, and the city attorney's office to catch proposals before they reach the council dais.

**Prince George's County and University of Maryland**

The county government and the university function as parallel power structures, often in tension with the city. The county controls school funding (as seen in the Paint Branch Chinese immersion fight), policing, and major infrastructure decisions. The University of Maryland shapes student housing markets, neighborhood character, and economic activity along Route 1. County officials like Councilmember Tom Dernoga and State Sen. Jim Rosapepe have opposed state housing reforms that College Park leaders might support. University partnerships matter for projects like the Lakeland murals and child care task force recommendations. Monitoring both institutions helps reporters understand when the city is pushing back, when it's collaborating, and when it's being overruled.

**Neighborhood Civic Associations — Berwyn, Calvert Hills, and Lakeland Groups**

These associations serve as early warning systems for resident discontent. The Berwyn District Civic Association and Calvert Hills Citizens Association have weighed in on development projects, school bus safety, and traffic concerns. Alexandra Tyukavina from the Paint Branch PTA proposed alternatives to cutting the Chinese immersion program. Donald Hays, chairman of a citizen-led school bus stop safety subcommittee, expressed outrage that the city ignored his recommendations. These voices often surface problems before they reach council meetings — parking disputes, unsafe sidewalks, noise complaints, or fears about "studentification" of established neighborhoods. Reporters should attend association meetings and track their communications with city officials to catch grassroots opposition or support for policy changes.

**Student Advocates and Tenant Organizers**

Students like Sami Saeed, Nisha Kumar, and Scott Tebbe have brought tenant concerns directly to the council, testifying about aggressive lease renewal practices and early lease pressure. Frances Chen, Nicholas Crane, and Ashna Pandya presented research on easing neighborhood tensions near the university. These voices represent a transient but significant constituency — one that shapes housing markets, neighborhood dynamics, and economic development along Route 1. Student organizers often lack institutional power but carry moral authority when they testify about exploitation or exclusion. They are essential for stories about housing affordability, tenant rights, and the evolving relationship between the university and the city.

**High-Frequency Sources Requiring Independent Verification**

Several sources appear frequently in the archive but should be approached with caution. Developers and landlords present their projects in the best possible light — Article Student Living frames its Guilford Drive proposal as community-responsive, but reporters should verify affordability claims, construction timelines, and community input independently. City staff like Khokhar and Williams advocate for administrative goals; their recommendations deserve scrutiny, not stenography. Even well-meaning advocates like Donald Hays may overstate urgency or gloss over complicating factors. Cross-check their assertions with city records, independent experts, or dissenting voices. The beat demands skepticism layered over access.
