## Executive Snapshot

# Executive Snapshot

Fazlul Kabir's name appears in 82 of these 266 stories — more than double any other figure on the College Park city council beat. This is not an accident of coverage. The mayor who has served six terms on council before leading the city has become the central character in a municipal drama playing out along Route 1, where a college town of 35,000 is trying to decide what it wants to be when it grows up. Will College Park become a national model for youth voting rights after lowering its voting age to 16? Will it force landlords to stop pressuring University of Maryland students into lease renewals before they've unpacked their first boxes? Will it finally reckon with the displaced residents of Lakeland — the historically Black neighborhood bulldozed between 1969 and 1981 — and provide the reparations a 20-member commission has recommended? These archived stories capture a city government wrestling with its identity: part university town navigating "studentification" tensions, part bedroom community watching single-family homes flip to rentals, part test lab for progressive policy experiments that range from expanding permit parking to exploring whether it needs its own police force.

The council itself has been in flux. Three newcomers — Daniel Oates, Kelly Jordan, and Holly Simmons — arrived after elections that brought younger voices to a dais already populated by members like Jacob Hernandez and Ray Ranker. Jordan won her District 2 seat by three votes after a hand recount. Now ten murals will tell that story on neighborhood walls — a gesture toward memory, if not yet justice.

What emerges from this archive is a city caught between its university and its neighborhoods, between renters and homeowners, between its progressive aspirations and its fiscal constraints. Affordable housing dominates the agenda — appearing in 30 stories — whether it's the 87-unit Branchville Crossing for seniors, the income-restricted Flats at College Park, or the fights over Governor Wes Moore's push for eight houses per acre that split local officials. Economic development means Wonder food halls opening on Baltimore Avenue and Article Student Living proposing 192 units on Guilford Drive. ## Top Recurring Issues

# Top Recurring Issues

**Affordable Housing and Tenant Protections**

The city's affordable housing crisis threads through nearly every housing conversation on this beat. With College Park caught between university expansion, private development pressures, and long-term residents priced out of their own neighborhoods, the council has moved aggressively on tenant protections — most notably the early lease ordinance preventing landlords from pressuring tenants to renew more than 180 days before lease end. Sami Saeed, a UMD public policy major, told the council how his landlord approached him just four weeks after moving in with a steep rent increase: "The stress that that caused me … trying to find anyone to live with for next year. I had to call 50 people." The ordinance passed unanimously, fining landlords $500 for first offenses and $2,500 after that. Meanwhile, developers like Article Student Living face pressure from Mayor Fazlul Kabir and Councilmember Denise Mitchell to include affordable units in new projects. Kabir has pitched the city's tax incentive program: "The catch is that you must have affordable housing in it." The city is also exploring Low-Income Housing Tax Credits to fund projects like Branchville Crossing, an 87-unit senior complex with rents pegged to 60% of area median income. Key players: Kabir, Mitchell, Kelly Jordan (who called senior housing "a real need"), Michael Williams (economic development director), and student advocates like Saeed.

**Restorative Justice for Lakeland**

The displacement of Lakeland residents during urban renewal from 1969 to 1981 remains unfinished business. The 20-member Restorative Justice Commission, chaired by Maxine Gross, has pushed the council to provide reparations — financial compensation, housing priority, free college tuition — to those who lived in Lakeland for at least 10 years or their descendants. "When you talk to people who experienced Lakeland before urban renewal, they speak of a vibrant community," Gross said. The commission's work intersects with the Lakeland Community Heritage Project, which is adding 10 murals to preserve the neighborhood's history. District 2 candidates Chris Gill, Kelly Jordan, and Holly Simmons all identified Lakeland reparations as a priority during their campaigns, with Simmons stating "reparations are very important." The issue keeps surfacing because displacement wasn't just policy — it was erasure, and the community hasn't let the city forget.

**Confidence: HIGH** — restorative justice appears as a tagged issue six times, plus extensive commission activity and candidate forum discussions.

**Student-Resident Tensions and "Studentification"**

The friction between students and long-term homeowners plays out on sidewalks, front lawns, and in council chambers. UMD students Frances Chen, Nicholas Crane, and Ashna Pandya presented research showing that "studentification" — the transformation of established neighborhoods into de facto campus housing — drives both high rents and neighbor complaints. Chen argued that "the most successful communities have used deep university and city cooperation to create a solution that works for both students and [homeowners]." Councilmember Alan Hew countered that the university should do more to teach students how to be good neighbors. The early lease ordinance addresses one piece — predatory lease renewals — while proposals like expanded permit parking tackle another. Former Councilmember Susan Whitney suggested incentivizing residents to own fewer cars, saying "we want people who can get by with one car to do that and to be encouraged to do that." The core tension: students need affordable housing, homeowners want stable neighborhoods, and landlords profit from the churn. No one's found the balance yet.

**Confidence: HIGH** — student housing appears repeatedly, plus community engagement issues and parking debates.

**Public Safety Without a City Police Force**

College Park doesn't have its own police department — Prince George's County handles law enforcement — but safety concerns keep the council searching for alternatives. District 3 candidates Dan Oates and Ray Ranker both rejected creating a city force, with Oates saying "jumping to the answer of 'we need a police force' is premature." Instead, the city has explored security cameras, blue light emergency phones, and stronger relationships with county and university police. Councilmember Alan Hew has become the council's de facto public safety expert, graduating from three citizen police academies (county, UMD, sheriff). "There's a lot of speculation of how they operate and I wanted to be able to speak confidently in support of the departments that operate within our city," Hew said. The vacant property registration ordinance — requiring owners to register empty buildings and pay $25 — also addresses safety, with Jacob Hernandez noting it "supports improving public safety" by tracking blight and vandalism risks. The debate continues: how do you make residents feel safe without building your own department?

**Why reporters should care:** This is the unresolved moral question shadowing every planning decision.
