## Top Recurring Issues

# Top Recurring Issues

**Affordable Housing and Development**

College Park wrestles constantly with its housing identity: a city caught between serving University of Maryland students and preserving space for families, between welcoming market-rate development and carving out homes people can actually afford. The friction shows up everywhere — in late-night council debates over student apartment towers, in negotiations with developers over how many units must be priced for moderate earners, in the painful math of whether a longtime resident can still afford to live here.

When Article Student Living proposed 192 units on Guilford Drive, city officials pressed hard: use the tax incentive program, but only if you include affordable housing. "We have a vision moving forward," Councilmember Denise Mitchell said, "and we're not going to negate that vision." Mayor Fazlul Kabir pitched the city's incentive program directly: "The catch is that you must have affordable housing in it." That exchange captures the city's posture — eager for development dollars, but increasingly unwilling to let them arrive without strings attached.

Elsewhere, the city has steered projects like Branchville Crossing toward seniors earning 60 percent of area median income, using federal low-income housing tax credits to make the numbers work. "There is a real need for senior housing in College Park," Councilmember Kelly Jordan said when that project moved forward. The Flats at College Park offers income-restricted units financed through similar federal credits. The city even runs Live+Work College Park, a zero-interest loan program for city employees buying homes — a small-bore attempt to keep people rooted here.

But state-level forces threaten local control. Governor Wes Moore pushed legislation to mandate eight houses per acre across Maryland, aiming to crack open zoning barriers statewide. House Speaker Joseline Peña-Melnyk, who represents College Park, backed the plan hard: "Maryland has a housing shortage of roughly 100,000 homes. We need more housing choices. Smaller homes. Townhouses. Accessory dwelling units." State Senator Jim Rosapepe and County Councilmember Tom Dernoga pushed back, citing congestion, pollution, and public safety — the old suburban anxieties about density.

For College Park, the question isn't abstract. As Christopher Gill, running for city council, put it: "I don't want people to be pushed out of their community." The city sits on a fulcrum: new construction could ease pressure, or accelerate displacement. Every project becomes a test case.

**Restorative Justice and the Lakeland Legacy**

No issue cuts deeper into College Park's moral ledger than what happened to Lakeland. Between 1969 and 1981, the city displaced a predominantly Black neighborhood through urban renewal — bulldozed homes, scattered families, erased a community. Decades later, the city is trying to reckon with that history, and the effort has moved from commemoration to compensation.

The 20-member Restorative Justice Commission, chaired by Maxine Gross, delivered recommendations that read like a to-do list for repair: financial compensation for displaced residents, housing priority, free college tuition for those who lived in Lakeland at least ten years or their direct descendants. "When you talk to people who experienced Lakeland before urban renewal," Gross said, "they speak of a vibrant community." Not past tense as nostalgia — past tense as evidence of what was taken.

The city has moved on smaller gestures. Lakeland is getting ten murals highlighting its history, a $28,382 project bringing together city officials, community members, and University of Maryland partners. "It's just a way for folks to be able to see the community that we have been talking about for a number of years," Gross said. Mayor Kabir called it "a wonderful and very easy and entertaining way to know about what happened and what used to be the historic Lakeland." The phrasing — "very easy" — suggests the gap between memorialization and restitution.

At a candidates' forum, District 2 hopefuls Holly Simmons, Kelly Jordan, and Chris Gill all agreed: restorative justice for Lakeland matters. Simmons said plainly, "Reparations are very important." Jordan suggested new developments include affordable units — an indirect way of addressing displacement's legacy. Whether the city follows through on the commission's bolder proposals — cash payments, housing guarantees, tuition waivers — remains unresolved. As of the archive, those recommendations sat before the council, waiting for a vote that would turn symbolism into policy.

**Student-Resident Tensions and Neighborhood Character**

The University of Maryland casts a long shadow, and beneath it, friction simmers. Students flood rental housing. Parties spill onto lawns. Cars pack neighborhood streets. Longtime homeowners feel their blocks slipping away — what urbanists call "studentification," the slow transformation of family neighborhoods into transient zones.

Three UMD students — Frances Chen, Nicholas Crane, Ashna Pandya — came to council to propose solutions, not just complaints. "We found that the most successful communities have used deep university and city cooperation to create a solution that works for both students and [homeowners]," Chen said, "and balances affordable housing for the student population [with] enforcing city codes." The presentation acknowledged high rents pushing students into single-family neighborhoods, but it also suggested the university could teach better neighboring. Councilmember Alan Hew agreed: the university should take more responsibility for teaching students how to live in a community.

Parking has become a proxy battlefield. The city considered expanding permit parking to keep non-resident cars off neighborhood streets. Parking Enforcement Officer Jim Miller proposed a study to figure out where restrictions made sense. "It's a matter of how that restricted parking is utilized," he said. Councilmembers debated whether to charge for permits. Former Councilmember Susan Whitney suggested tax breaks to nudge residents toward owning fewer cars: "We want people who need their cars to be able to park them, and we want people who can get by with one car to do that and to be encouraged to do that."

Noise ordinances, unruly social gathering fines, code enforcement — the city keeps tinkering with regulations, trying to find the dial setting that protects quality of life without punishing students for being young. But the deeper issue persists: College Park is two cities sharing one map, and no ordinance can fully reconcile them.

**Landlord Practices and Tenant Protections**

Landlords in College Park move fast, and students often get caught flat-footed. One policy fight crystallized the problem: the early lease ordinance, which prevents landlords from forcing tenants to commit to another year before they've even settled into their current apartment.

Sami Saeed, a UMD public policy major, told council about being approached by his landlord just four weeks into his lease — hit with a big rent increase and an ultimatum. "The stress that that caused me," he said, "trying to find anyone to live with for next year. I had to call 50 people." His story wasn't unique. Nearly 30 people, mostly student tenants, showed up to ask the council for protection. Nisha Kumar described the whiplash: "We've just moved in for the semester and we already have to figure out next year... Who's going to take my spot? Will I have to pay over $1,000 a month for a place I'm not even living in?" Scott Tebbe said simply, "This was a decision I wasn't ready to make, and I felt trapped."

The council passed the ordinance unanimously — landlords can't ask for lease renewals earlier than 180 days before the current lease ends. Violations bring a $500 fine first time, $2,500 after that. The ordinance was amended to count the window from the lease's end, not its beginning, tightening the protection. Landlords opposed it, arguing it limited their control over tenant selection, but council sided with renters.

This wasn't the only tenant issue bubbling up. The city also considered raising fines for code violations — construction, fire safety, housing, noise, unruly gatherings. Director of Public Services Jatinder Khokhar and City Attorney Stephanie Anderson pitched aligning city fines with state law, which allows municipalities to fine up to $5,000. Councilmember John Rigg noted the overlap: "Noise should be bad, but unruly social gatherings almost always include noise and it includes a lot more." The city's attempting to professionalize enforcement, make penalties sting enough to change behavior.

**Public Safety Without a City Police Force**

College Park doesn't have its own police department. It relies on Prince George's County Police and the University of Maryland Police. That arrangement keeps coming up — usually when someone gets hurt, or when residents feel unsafe, or when a candidate promises to do something about crime without quite saying what.

District 3 candidates Dan Oates and Ray Ranker discussed public safety during the campaign, both steering away from creating a city force. "I think jumping to the answer of 'we need a police force' is premature," Oates said. Ranker suggested other measures: more security cameras, blue light phones, better coordination with existing departments. The conversation reflects a broader ambivalence — residents want safety, but not necessarily more police.

Councilmember Alan Hew has become the council's unofficial liaison to law enforcement, graduating from three different citizen police academies: Prince George's County Police, UMD Police, and the Prince George's County Sheriff's Office. "There's a lot of speculation of how they operate," Hew said, "and I wanted to be able to speak confidently in support of the departments that operate within our city." His training lets him translate between police operations and resident concerns, bridge the knowledge gap when safety debates flare up.

But the city's safety strategy relies on tools beyond policing: code enforcement against problem properties, coordination with the university on student conduct, lighting and infrastructure improvements, vacant property registries. The city passed an ordinance requiring owners of vacant homes and commercial buildings to register with the city, pay $25, and face fines for non-compliance. Mayor Kabir framed it as prevention: "Vacant properties can negatively impact our community. When left unmonitored, they can become safety hazards, attract vandalism and contribute to blight." Councilmember Jacob Hernandez agreed: "It supports improving public safety. It protects property values."

The absence of a city police force isn't an oversight — it's a choice, sustained by budget constraints and political will. But every crime, every late-night disturbance, every safety scare reopens the question.

**City-University Relationship and Economic Development**

The University of Maryland is College Park's economic engine and its complication. The relationship isn't hostile, but it's complicated — two large institutions sharing tight geography, each with its own priorities, forced into constant negotiation.

Economic development runs through this channel. When Wonder opened its first Maryland food hall on Baltimore Avenue, city leaders celebrated it as proof College Park could attract national brands. Councilmember John Rigg said, "The city has been working hard to bring an exciting concept here. I think they hit it out of the park." Mayor Kabir called it "a milestone" and noted the location as "the epicenter of downtown College Park." Economic development director Michael Williams emphasized the fit: a variety of restaurants in a high-traffic student area. The project worked because the city and university populations overlapped — students need food, the city needs tax revenue and street life.

But the university also generates problems the city inherits. When Frances Chen, Nicholas Crane, and Ashna Pandya presented on neighborhood tensions, they pointed to the need for "deep university and city cooperation." The university's enrollment drives housing demand, which drives rent increases, which pushes students into neighborhoods never designed for them. The university doesn't pay property taxes. Its students don't all register to vote locally, diluting resident political power. Its growth plans don't always sync with the city's.

Route 1 and Baltimore Avenue run like a commercial spine through both territories. When the city talks about streetscape improvements, traffic calming, or mixed-use development, it's trying to shape a corridor the university also claims. Projects like the Old Calvert Road bridge — which would connect Old Calvert Road Park to the Northeast Branch Trail — reflect efforts to knit the city together, make it more than a backdrop for campus life. Councilmember Ray Ranker said the community had been asking for that bridge for at least ten years: "We don't want to spend another month waiting, we want to get [construction] started tomorrow." The $2 million price tag and decade-long wait suggest the challenge of turning aspiration into infrastructure.

The university employs thousands, educates tens of thousands, attracts research dollars and cultural events. The city needs it. But the relationship requires constant tending — coordination on student conduct, housing supply, public safety, transportation, economic opportunity. When it works, both benefit. When it doesn't, residents feel overrun and students feel unwelcome.

**Infrastructure, Transportation, and Traffic Calming**

College Park's streets weren't built for the volume they carry now. The city keeps trying to calm traffic, add sidewalks, fix dangerous intersections — incremental repairs to a system under constant strain.

A citizen-led committee resubmitted a report on school bus stop safety, recommending upgrades at seven stops that scored poorly on safety evaluations. Committee chairman Donald Hays said bluntly, "I personally feel that is an outrage and there has been very little movement and very little progress." The recommendations included bike lanes and sidewalks — basic infrastructure to protect elementary school kids waiting for buses. That the city hadn't acted after receiving the report once already suggests how these projects stall.

Noise complaints on Edmonston Road revealed another infrastructure failure. After the city removed trees between Edmonston Road and Kenilworth Avenue, traffic noise became "clearly audible in the front yards," according to a city-commissioned analysis by RK&K. Resident Maria Cordone and others complained. The analysis recommended a more detailed study and potential mitigation by the State Highway Administration. Councilmember Ray Ranker said the city planned to plant trees and shrubs to help with sound and beautification, a provisional fix while waiting on the state. Some residents wanted traffic-calming devices to stop speeders — another request that requires coordination beyond city limits.

Speed bumps split the council. Councilmember John Rigg, who also volunteers as a paramedic, opposed speed humps on Autoville Drive based on his experience transporting patients: "When you have a long bone fracture, especially like a hip or a pelvis, which happen more often than you think, speed bumps can cause pain." He suggested a sidewalk instead. The debate reflects the tradeoffs: slow cars to protect residents, but not so aggressively you hurt people in ambulances.

The Old Calvert Road bridge represents the bigger ambition — not just fixing problems but adding connections. Residents had requested it for at least a decade. The estimated cost ran around $2 million, and the city considered a cheaper option to modify the Campus Drive bridge instead. Councilmember Ranker pushed to start construction immediately. As of the archive, the council had endorsed the project, but actual construction hadn't begun — the familiar gap between approval and groundbreaking.

Micromobility added another layer. Veo scooters and bikes saw a 62 percent ridership increase between fall 2024 and fall 2025, which sounds good until scooters start cluttering sidewalks and doorways. Veo implemented stricter parking rules, charging riders $4 to $5 extra for parking outside designated areas and fines up to $60 for repeat violations. Mayor Kabir supported the program as aligned with sustainability goals: "Every time someone rides a Veo scooter, that person is not using the car, right? So we're taking out the cars from the street." But the scooters created their own infrastructure problem — where to park them, how to keep them from blocking pedestrians, how to balance convenience with order.

**Child Care Costs and Availability**

College Park families face a child care crisis that bleeds into every other decision — where to live, whether to work, how many kids to have. The city formed a Child Care Task Force to figure out solutions, and the resulting recommendations read like an economic development plan and a family support program rolled into one.

The task force, which included University of Maryland public policy student Gavin Neubauer, proposed grants to home-based child care providers and property tax credits to established centers. "If you're able to even increase the amount of businesses here that can survive and care for children," Neubauer said, "we're going to be a real standout city in this entire metropolitan area." The report offered nearly 20 actions to lower costs and increase availability in a city with just 18 licensed providers: 11 day care centers, five home-based providers, two tax-exempt religious organizations offering after-school care. That's a thin network for a city College Park's size.

Child care is expensive infrastructure — staff, training, facilities, insurance. It doesn't generate tax revenue the way a restaurant or retail shop does. Providers operate on tight margins, vulnerable to rent increases and regulatory costs. Families who can't find affordable care either leave College Park or stop working, which shrinks the tax base and undermines the city's efforts to retain residents long-term.

The task force's proposals acknowledge this — public subsidy is necessary because the private market alone won't supply enough care at prices families can afford. Whether the city follows through with grants and tax credits depends on budget priorities, but the task force framed child care as essential infrastructure, not optional amenity. If College Park wants to be a place where families stay, it needs to solve this.

**Political Participation and the Expanded Franchise**

College Park lowered its voting age to 16 for city elections, joining eight other Maryland municipalities in expanding the franchise to teenagers. The 5-3 council vote made national headlines, but locally it carried different weight — a bet that civic engagement starts earlier when you give young people stakes in local decisions.

Lucas Rigg, a 16-year-old resident, said simply, "I think it lets the people who didn't choose to live here have a say." Lovisa Jordan, also 16, called it "cool" and noted that preparing to vote could make teenagers more aware of what's happening in the city: "I think that it's an option that we can get our voices and opinions heard." The first election after the change saw six teenagers aged 16 and 17 cast ballots — a small number, but a start.

The move reflects a broader question about who gets to shape College Park's future. Students live here temporarily but dominate the population. Families raising kids have long-term stakes but less political voice if they're outnumbered. Expanding the voting age gives teenagers who live in College Park — many of them children of homeowners — a formal channel to influence decisions that will affect them for years, even if they eventually leave for college.

Voter turnout remains low overall. In one November election, just 1,598 people voted — 9.9 percent of registered voters. Mayor Fazlul Kabir won with 1,480 votes. Councilmembers Jacob Hernandez and Alan Hew led their races with 536 and 492 votes respectively. These numbers suggest that expanding participation matters not just symbolically but practically — every additional voter shifts the balance in tight races.

The council also wrestled with its own compensation, considering doubling part-time salaries from $10,000 to $20,000 annually. Councilmember Jacob Hernandez argued, "Everyone on this dais works incredibly hard for the residents that we care for," and noted the proposed increase "might not be extraordinarily significant, but it does help cover the costs." Councilmember Ray Ranker agreed: "If the pay was $1 million, more people would probably run for office than if the pay were $0 per year." The discussion wasn't about enriching incumbents — it was about making council service accessible to people who can't afford to work for peanuts. A city council dominated by retirees and people with flexible jobs doesn't represent the full community. Better pay could diversify who runs.

These debates — voting age, council compensation, turnout, representation — circle the same question: who participates in College Park's democracy, and what barriers keep others out?

## Timeline and Trend Shifts

# Timeline and Trend Shifts

The College Park beat has moved through distinct phases, each leaving sediment in the political landscape. What began as scattered community concerns has hardened into structural conflicts about who the city serves and what it owes to those it has displaced.

## The Lakeland Reckoning

The transformation started with murals. Ten of them, planned for the Lakeland neighborhood, seemed like a straightforward heritage project — a way to preserve stories of a historically Black community that urban renewal had fractured between 1969 and 1981. Maxine Gross spoke of helping people "see the community that we have been talking about for a number of years." But murals turned out to be prologue.

By the time the Restorative Justice Commission delivered its recommendations, the ask had sharpened into something the city couldn't simply commemorate away: financial reparations, housing priority, free college tuition for anyone who lived in Lakeland for at least a decade or their direct descendants. "When you talk to people who experienced Lakeland before urban renewal, they speak of a vibrant community," Gross said. The commission had 20 members. The proposals landed before a council that would have to decide whether acknowledgment required restitution.

This thread hasn't resolved. A reporter should verify where the reparations debate stands now, whether the council has acted, and how the displaced Lakelanders and their descendants are pressing the issue. The tension between symbolic gestures and material justice runs through other housing debates on this beat.

## The Student Housing Pressure Cooker

The early lease ordinance tells you everything about power dynamics between landlords and students in a college town. Sami Saeed got approached four weeks after moving in — four weeks — with a renewal demand and a rent hike. He called 50 people trying to sort out next year's living situation. Nearly 30 people, mostly students, showed up at council to ask for protection from what they called predatory practices.

The ordinance passed unanimously: landlords couldn't demand lease renewals sooner than 180 days before the current lease ended. First offense, $500 fine. Repeat violations, $2,500. Nisha Kumar had captured the absurdity: "We've just moved in for the semester and we already have to figure out next year... Who's going to take my spot? ... Will I have to pay over $1,000 a month for a place I'm not even living in?"

But the student housing conversation didn't stop at lease timing. Frances Chen, Nicholas Crane, and Ashna Pandya came to council with a broader diagnosis: "studentification" of established neighborhoods, high rents pushing people out, inadequate university responsibility for teaching undergraduates how to be decent neighbors. Chen pointed to successful college towns that had used "deep university and city cooperation" to balance affordability with code enforcement. Alan Hew agreed the university needed to step up.

Then Article Student Living proposed a 192-unit complex on Guilford Drive, replacing Hope Lutheran Church. The church would get new space in the building — larger, fully accessible, maybe room for offices or affordable housing. Fazlul Kabir pushed the developer toward the city's tax incentive program, which required affordable units. Denise Mitchell made it clear: "We have a vision moving forward, and we're not going to negate that vision."

The pattern: students organizing around tenant protections, the council responding with ordinances, developers getting pressed to include affordability even in market-rate projects. The University of Maryland looms over 94 stories in this dataset, but the tension is less about town-gown cooperation than about the city trying to regulate a rental market the university's enrollment shapes but doesn't control. Verify whether Article built that project and whether it included affordable units as requested.

## Affordable Housing as Persistent Demand

Affordable housing appears 30 times if you count the tags, but the real number is higher because it threads through student housing debates, senior housing proposals, the Lakeland reparations push, and nearly every development conversation.

Branchville Crossing showed the council at its most unified on this issue: an 87-unit apartment building for residents 62 and older, rent based on 60 percent of area median income, financed through federal low-income housing tax credits. Kelly Jordan said it plainly: "There is a real need for senior housing in College Park." The developer had listened to residents, adjusted plans. The council approved the parking variance needed to make it work.

The Flats at College Park offered 317 units with income restrictions, financed partly through Low-Income Housing Tax Credit programs. The city runs a Community Preservation Trust that sells single-family homes to eligible buyers. There's a Live+Work College Park program offering zero-interest loans to city employees who buy homes here — a retention tool dressed up as affordability policy.

But then came Governor Wes Moore's proposal to change Maryland zoning law, requiring municipalities to allow at least eight houses per acre to address the state's 100,000-home shortage. House Speaker Joseline Peña-Melnyk — a College Park resident and former councilmember — championed it. "We need more housing choices. Smaller homes. Townhouses. Accessory dwelling units," she said.

State Senator Jim Rosapepe and Prince George's County Councilmember Tom Dernoga pushed back, Dernoga citing concerns about congestion, pollution, public safety. The fault line: state mandate versus local control, density versus neighborhood character, regional housing crisis versus municipal autonomy.

A reporter needs to track where that legislation landed and whether College Park has changed its zoning in response. The city keeps saying it wants affordable housing, keeps requiring it in new developments, but the structural question — how much density to allow, who decides, what gets demolished or transformed — remains contested.

Three newcomers elected to council — Daniel Oates, Kelly Jordan, Holly Simmons — joined younger existing members like Jacob Hernandez and Ray Ranker. Mitchell's declaration that the city has "a vision moving forward, and we're not going to negate that vision" captures a council increasingly willing to push developers toward affordability requirements and reject proposals that don't fit its emerging priorities.

But Llatetra Brown Esters and Susan Whitney left the council, their District 2 seats filled by Jordan and Simmons after a tight race. Patrick Wojahn appears in 12 stories — verify whether he's still mayor of neighboring College Park or whether that's historical. Joseline Peña-Melnyk moved from the House of Delegates to become Speaker, making her the most powerful College Park resident in state politics.

The turnover creates both opportunity and risk. New members bring fresh perspectives, ask new questions, as Oates noted. But institutional memory fades, developer relationships reset, long-term projects lose champions.

## Where the Beat Stands Now

A reporter covering College Park today inherits a council that has moved from reactive governance to setting conditions — on lease renewals, on affordable housing requirements, on vacant property registration, on teen voting rights. The Lakeland reparations debate has elevated a historical injustice into a current political demand that will define how seriously the city takes equity work.

The University of Maryland remains the largest presence and the most ambiguous partner. Student tenant organizing has proven effective, but the broader town-gown relationship needs mapping. Affordable housing has become the council's stated priority, yet zoning changes and density debates remain contested.

Infrastructure projects like the Old Calvert Road bridge and school bus stop safety improvements reveal how long it takes to move from community asks to actual construction. The council's willingness to double its own pay and lower the voting age suggests a body rethinking democratic participation, but 9.9 percent turnout suggests residents aren't yet engaged.

The gaps matter: arts and culture policy, climate adaptation beyond micromobility, transparent budget processes, economic development strategy beyond opportunistic developer negotiations. And the unresolved questions matter more: Did the council act on Lakeland reparations? Did the vacant property registry change anything? Did Article build that student housing with affordable units? Did the state's density mandate pass, and did College Park comply?

The beat has evolved from managing routine municipal concerns to confronting structural questions about equity, affordability, and who gets to shape the city's future. The council has tools — ordinances, zoning leverage, tax incentives — but the will to use them consistently, and the political capital to withstand developer and state pressure, will determine whether College Park's vision becomes reality or remains rhetoric.

**The Trump Effect One Year Later: Who Actually Got Help?**  
A year into Trump's second term, College Park faced layoffs, funding cuts, and uncertainty. The story of Trump's impact on College Park shouldn't stop at "we do not know how things will actually go" and the announcement of a boosted assistance fund. It should follow individual families navigating the wreckage, track whether the city's safety net held, and examine whether federal cuts changed the city's tax base, school enrollment, or housing market. The downstream effects of a federal workforce contraction in a city adjacent to federal research facilities — that's a story that needs shoe-leather reporting, not just a policy announcement.

Did it start? Is the bridge open, or did cost overruns, engineering snags, or county coordination issues stall it?

**Why now:** Residents have waited 10+ years; they deserve a progress report. Infrastructure projects reveal whether the council can deliver on big promises.

**Who to call:** Councilmember Ray Ranker, public works director, Maryland-National Capital Park and Planning Commission, neighbors, trail users, budget director.

**Status check:** Verify construction status. If delayed, get updated cost estimates and timeline. If complete, assess community usage.

**Why now:** Fine increases either deter violations or spark pushback from landlords and businesses. Enforcement data should reveal the policy's real impact.

**Who to call:** Councilmember John Rigg, Director of Public Services Jatinder Khokhar, City Attorney Stephanie Anderson, code enforcement staff, landlords, student renters.

**Status check:** Verify ordinance passage and get violation/fine data since implementation. Track whether complaints decreased or fine revenue increased.

## 24. The Speed Bump Paramedic Problem
Paramedic and Councilmember John Rigg opposed speed humps on Autoville Drive because they cause pain to patients with hip or pelvis fractures. He suggested sidewalks instead. What got built? Did Rigg's dual expertise — 16 years with Calvert Advanced Life Support — override neighbors' traffic-calming demands, or did safety concerns force compromise?

**Why now:** Speed humps versus patient comfort is a rare policy collision worth examining. Rigg's credibility on emergency medicine gives him unusual leverage.

**Who to call:** Councilmember John Rigg, CALS colleagues, Autoville Drive residents, public works/traffic engineers, other council members who voted on the measure.

**Status check:** Visit Autoville Drive or get city records on what traffic-calming measures were installed. Track accident or speeding data before and after.
